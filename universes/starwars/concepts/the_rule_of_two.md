# The Rule of Two

## Overview

"Always two there are. No more, no less. A master and an apprentice." Yoda spoke these words upon learning a Sith Lord had resurfaced after a millennium of presumed extinction. The Rule of Two is the organizational doctrine that allowed the Sith to survive, hide, and ultimately triumph — not through armies of dark side users, but through patience, secrecy, and the concentrated transfer of power across a thousand years.

## Key Facts

[spoiler_tier: prequel_trilogy]
- **Darth Bane's doctrine:** Roughly a thousand years before the fall of the Republic, the Sith were numerous and constantly at war — with the Jedi and with each other. Darth Bane recognized that the Sith's greatest enemy was their own nature: multiple Sith inevitably turned on each other, weakening the order. He destroyed the existing Sith and established the Rule of Two: one master to embody power, one apprentice to crave it. [film_pt] [legends]
- **The logic:** The master trains the apprentice. The apprentice grows stronger. Eventually, the apprentice challenges the master. If the apprentice wins, they are stronger — the Sith grow more powerful. If the master wins, the apprentice was unworthy, and the master finds a new one. Either outcome strengthens the Sith lineage. Survival of the fittest, applied to a religion. [legends]
- **Hiding in plain sight:** The Rule of Two's greatest advantage was invisibility. Two Sith could hide where a thousand could not. For a millennium, the Jedi believed the Sith extinct. Palpatine served as a senator, then chancellor, then emperor — a Sith Lord operating at the heart of the Republic while the Jedi Temple stood across the plaza. [film_pt]
- **The Palpatine lineage:** Darth Plagueis trained Darth Sidious (Palpatine). Sidious murdered Plagueis in his sleep — the Rule in action. Sidious then trained Darth Maul as his apprentice while still an apprentice himself (a violation, or a pragmatic adaptation). After Maul's apparent death, he took Dooku. After Dooku's death, he took Anakin. Each apprentice served a specific purpose in the grand plan. [film_pt]

[spoiler_tier: clone_wars_era]
- **Maul's complication:** Darth Maul survived being bisected by Obi-Wan Kenobi, rebuilt himself with mechanical legs, and declared himself a Sith Lord. Sidious did not agree. When Maul and his brother Savage Opress grew too powerful, Sidious visited them personally and killed Savage while subduing Maul. "You have become a rival," Sidious told him. The Rule of Two does not tolerate rivals. [tv_tcw]
- **Ventress and the betrayal cycle:** Asajj Ventress served as Count Dooku's dark side assassin — not a Sith, technically, but trained enough to raise Sidious's suspicion. Sidious ordered Dooku to kill Ventress, fearing she was becoming a de facto third Sith. Dooku obeyed, betraying Ventress. She survived and became a bounty hunter. The Rule generated disposable weapons: trained enough to be useful, never invested in enough to be kept. [tv_tcw]
- **Savage Opress:** Ventress, seeking revenge on Dooku, had Mother Talzin transform the Nightbrother Savage Opress into a dark side weapon. Savage was briefly Dooku's "apprentice" — making three active dark-siders simultaneously. The instability was immediate. The Rule of Two was designed precisely to prevent this kind of chaotic multiplication. [tv_tcw]

[spoiler_tier: everything]
- **Why it worked:** The Jedi Order had thousands of members, a Temple, a Council, and galactic visibility. The Sith had two people and a plan. The Jedi grew complacent through institutional inertia. The Sith grew sharper through existential pressure. A thousand years of accumulated knowledge, hatred, and strategic planning flowed through two vessels at a time — until the vessel named Palpatine was ready to pour it all out at once. [film_pt] [legends]
- **The end of the Rule:** Vader killed Palpatine to save Luke, dying in the process. Both master and apprentice destroyed each other — the Rule of Two's ultimate expression. Whether Palpatine's return on Exegol violated or continued the Rule is a matter of perspective. [film_ot] [film_st]
