# Arrakis

## Overview

Arrakis is the third planet of Canopus, also called Dune — the desert world, the only known source of spice melange in the universe. It is an arid planet of extreme conditions: surface temperatures reaching seventy degrees Celsius in the equatorial zones, sandstorms of such force and scale that they can strip flesh from bone and flatten structures, and beneath the sands, the greatest organisms in the known universe. It should not be habitable. Several million Fremen have made it home.

It is the most politically contested piece of real estate in human space. The Imperium is held together in large part by the spice that comes from here. Every Guild Navigator, every aging aristocrat drinking spice coffee, every Bene Gesserit Reverend Mother — all of them depend on what this desert planet produces.

## Key Facts

- **Location:** Third planet of Canopus. [book_dune]
- **Surface conditions:** Extreme aridity. Almost no surface water. Temperatures capable of killing unprotected humans within hours. High-velocity sandstorms. Seasonal storms called Coriolis storms in polar regions. [book_dune]
- **Sandworms:** The great worms — Shai-Hulud — can reach hundreds of meters in length. They are attracted to rhythmic vibration (footsteps, machinery, anything regular). Their territory covers most of the planet's surface. They cannot tolerate standing water. The Fremen call them "makers" because they are essential to the spice cycle. [book_dune]
- **The spice cycle:** Sandworm larvae — called sandtrout — colonize water deposits beneath the sand, sealing them into ecological dead zones. When sandtrout masses accumulate and are then released by some disruption, the resulting reaction with the soil produces a spice blow — an eruption of spice melange at the surface. The worms are attracted to the blow. If a worm reaches the blow, it destroys the spice. Harvesters must extract the spice and evacuate before the worm arrives. [book_dune]
- **Polar regions:** The poles and high latitudes have enough moisture for some limited conventional plant life. The ecological transformation project that Liet-Kynes has been superintending focuses on these regions as the starting point — building enough plant cover to create moisture traps, expanding outward over centuries. [book_dune]
- **Sietch system:** The Fremen live in underground communities — sietches — distributed across the deep desert. The locations of sietches are secret. Anyone who knows a sietch location and is not of the sietch must be killed; the knowledge is too dangerous. [book_dune]

## The Trap

Arrakis is offered to House Atreides as a gift and is a carefully constructed death trap. The Harkonnen left operational problems embedded in the spice infrastructure. The political situation is hostile. The Fremen are an unknown quantity that turns out to be far more significant than outsider intelligence suggested.

The planet itself is the weapon. An off-world force trying to pacify Arrakis is fighting the terrain, the ecology, the climate, and a population that has spent ten thousand years learning to live in all of it. The Harkonnens never actually controlled Arrakis. They controlled the settled areas and the spice harvesting infrastructure. Everything else belonged to the Fremen. [book_dune] [film_2021]

## The Ecological Dream

Liet-Kynes spent his life pursuing the Fremen's centuries-old dream: transforming Arrakis into a green world. The project is not fantasy. It has been underway for generations. Windtraps collect atmospheric moisture. Sheltered plantings expand into protected valleys. The calculations, done correctly, show that Arrakis could be transformed — over three or four hundred years of patient, sustained work — into a planet with open water, vegetation, and survivable ambient conditions.

The sandworms would not survive this transformation. Spice production would end. The Fremen religious complex frames this as the paradise at the end of prophecy. The ecological science frames it as possible. The Sisterhood, and the Guild, and everyone else who depends on spice production would regard it as catastrophic.

Paul inherits this dream and this dilemma. The future he controls — and partly causes — includes both the potential transformation and the reasons why transformation may never be permitted. [book_dune]
