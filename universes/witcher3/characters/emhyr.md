# Emhyr var Emreis

## Overview

Emhyr var Emreis, styled the White Flame Dancing on the Barrows of his Enemies, is the Emperor of Nilfgaard — the most powerful political figure on the Continent and the man whose military campaigns have brought the Northern Kingdoms to the edge of collapse. He is cold, precise, calculating, and operates with a long time horizon that most people cannot match. He is also, in one of the Witcher saga's most significant reveals, the man who was once Duny — the cursed Urcheon of Erlenwald, whose life Geralt saved decades ago using the Law of Surprise that bound Geralt to Ciri. He is Ciri's father. These two facts — emperor of the invading power and Ciri's father — coexist without easily resolving. He loves Ciri, in whatever way a man who weaponizes everything is capable of love. He also intends to use her.

## Key Facts

- **Title:** Emhyr var Emreis, the White Flame Dancing on the Barrows of his Enemies, Emperor of Nilfgaard [books] [game_w3]
- **Former identity:** Duny, also known as the Urcheon of Erlenwald — an Nilfgaardian prince cursed into a hedgehog-man form, whom Geralt encountered years before the main game. [books]
- **Relation to Ciri:** Biological father. Ciri's mother was Pavetta of Cintra. [books] [game_w3]
- **Relation to Geralt:** Emhyr is the man whose life Geralt saved, inadvertently claiming Ciri through the Law of Surprise. He is simultaneously the reason Geralt has Ciri as a daughter and the man Geralt's working for at the start of Witcher 3. [books] [game_w3]
- **Appearance:** Older man, well-maintained. Military bearing. Dressed simply for an emperor — practical rather than ostentatious. The authority comes from presence, not ornamentation. [game_w3]
- **Intelligence:** Very high. He is not deceived easily. He is aware of more than he reveals in any given conversation. [books] [game_w3]
- **The curse (backstory):** Duny was an Nilfgaardian prince whose father was deposed. He was cursed to take hedgehog form from dusk to dawn. Geralt's intervention at a Cintra feast broke the curse — the twist being that Pavetta's love for Duny (and her own inherited Elder Blood power) was the true mechanism of breaking it. Geralt invoked the Law of Surprise afterward, claiming Duny's unknowing child — which turned out to be Ciri. [books]
- **The throne:** Emhyr reclaimed the Nilfgaardian throne through a coup, killing those who had deposed his father. By the time of the games, he has ruled for decades and transformed Nilfgaard into the dominant continental power. [books]

## Relationships

- **Ciri:** His daughter. His intention is to use her Elder Blood lineage to legitimize his rule and produce an heir that continues the dynastic line. The books are explicit that he intended to marry her — a fact that the games acknowledge obliquely but do not dwell on, as it is one of the story's most disturbing elements. His feelings for Ciri are genuine within a framework that also treats her as a political resource. He is not incapable of love; he is incapable of separating love from utility. [books] [game_w3]
- **Geralt:** Employs him. Respects his capability in the way of someone who uses tools he recognizes as quality. Their history (Duny/the Law of Surprise) is not directly addressed in their in-game interactions, but Emhyr knows what Geralt is to Ciri and has calculated accordingly. [books] [game_w3]
- **Yennefer:** Uses her. She is working under his authority during Witcher 3 and she knows exactly what the relationship is. There is no pretense of loyalty on either side. [game_w3]
- **His generals and court:** Emhyr operates through layers of subordinates who carry out policy. He does not need to be brutal personally because his system is brutal efficiently. [books]

## Velen and Novigrad Context

Emhyr's presence in the game is felt more than seen in the early arcs. He is the authority figure behind Yennefer's mission. He is why Geralt has resources and access that a freelance witcher would not. His interest in Ciri's location is the official framing for the entire search — even if Geralt's personal motivation is very different.

His occupation of Velen (through the Nilfgaardian garrison) is the ground-level reality of his empire. The soldiers at Velen's fortified position represent what Emhyr's expansion looks like from beneath — soldiers with authority and limited accountability, occupying land that was Temerian a few years ago.

The Imperial messenger who summons Geralt to Vizima at the game's start represents Emhyr's first direct touch on the plot. The meeting at Vizima — brief, controlled, Emhyr revealing his objectives — establishes his character immediately: precise language, clear priorities, the use of Yennefer as a point of emotional leverage (she is present, which is both a practical convenience and a calculation). ## Skellige and Beyond

Emhyr has interests on Skellige but does not appear personally. His political maneuvering in the succession crisis — attempting to position a favorable jarl through agents — is background to the main Skellige arc. His goal: a Skellige leadership that will not actively oppose Nilfgaard, ideally one that can be managed.

[spoiler_tier: kaer_morhen]
## Kaer Morhen Events

Emhyr is not present at Kaer Morhen. The Wild Hunt is a separate threat from Nilfgaard, and Emhyr's response to the Wild Hunt threat to Ciri is pragmatic: he wants Ciri found by Geralt, which means the Wild Hunt should not have her. He is on the same side as Geralt regarding the immediate objective (Ciri's safety from the Hunt) without being an ally in any meaningful sense.

After Kaer Morhen and the beginning of the final arc, Emhyr's role becomes explicit: Ciri will either come to him or she won't. He has played his part — hiring Geralt, maintaining the resources — and now waits. [game_w3]

## Endings and Resolution

**If Ciri meets Emhyr (quest "Blood on the Battlefield"):**
Geralt can take Ciri to meet her father. The meeting is controlled on Emhyr's side: he receives her, recognizes her, makes his offer — Empress of Nilfgaard, continuation of his dynasty, the legitimacy she carries by Elder Blood. He does not force the choice; he presents it. Whether Ciri accepts depends on player choices throughout the game.

**If Ciri becomes Empress:**
Emhyr steps back. This is the plan succeeding. He cedes the throne to his daughter and the implication is that he retires or dies in the years following. He got what he wanted. The question of what he wanted exactly — to control Ciri or to give his daughter a future — is left deliberately ambiguous. The answer is probably both, inextricably.

**If Ciri does not become Empress:**
Emhyr accepts this also. He is not shown losing his composure over the failure of his dynastic plan. He is too controlled for that. The war continues; his empire continues. He simply does not have an heir with Elder Blood.

**If Radovid is assassinated and the political consequences unfold:**
Emhyr's Nilfgaard wins the war in most of the political endings. Whether Temeria gets autonomy or not (depending on whether the Temerian resistance or Dijkstra survives the post-Radovid chaos), Nilfgaard emerges dominant. Emhyr has been playing a longer game than any other faction. He generally wins it. [game_w3]

## DLC Content

Emhyr does not appear in the DLCs. The Hearts of Stone and Blood and Wine storylines are personal and local to Geralt — O'Dimm's supernatural dealings and Toussaint's vampire crisis are outside the scope of imperial politics. Emhyr's empire is background noise in both DLCs, present in the formal political structure of Toussaint as a Nilfgaardian vassal duchy but not in any active sense. [game_w3]
