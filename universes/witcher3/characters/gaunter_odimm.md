# Gaunter O'Dimm

## Overview

Gaunter O'Dimm — also known as Master Mirror, the Merchant of Mirrors, the Man of Glass, and the Evil Incarnate — is the central antagonist of the Hearts of Stone DLC and arguably the most dangerous entity Geralt encounters across all three games. He appears briefly in the main game as a helpful stranger who gives Geralt directions in White Orchard — an appearance so mundane that it is almost certainly deliberate misdirection. He is helpful, charming, precise with language, and at no point in the entirety of his story does he do anything that he did not intend to do. His nature is left deliberately ambiguous: not a demon in the conventional sense, not the devil of any recognizable tradition, something older and stranger that has made agreements, eaten souls, and walked the Continent (and perhaps other worlds) for longer than most civilizations have existed.

## Key Facts

- **Known names:** Gaunter O'Dimm, Master Mirror, Man of Glass, Merchant of Mirrors, the Evil Incarnate [game_w3]
- **Nature:** Supernatural entity of undefined classification. Not a vampire, not a djinn, not a demon in any canonical Witcher sense. Something that precedes or exists outside those categories. [game_w3]
- **Power:** Apparently near-absolute within the rules of his contracts. He can alter reality, freeze time, create pocket dimensions, and enforce deals in ways that suggest either divinity or something adjacent to it. He appears to operate under self-imposed rules — the deal structure, the riddle system — that he genuinely cannot break. [game_w3]
- **The mirror:** His title "Master Mirror" and "Merchant of Mirrors" refers to the fact that a mirror reflects what is there but has no image of its own. This is central to his riddle's answer. [game_w3]
- **The main game appearance:** In White Orchard, early in Witcher 3, Geralt encounters a friendly man at a roadside who gives him directions to the inn. This is O'Dimm. He is not flagged as significant. This is one of the most effective foreshadowing moments in the game — you only understand what you saw when you've finished Hearts of Stone. [game_w3]
- **Contract structure:** O'Dimm trades in deals. He provides what is asked for; the cost is a soul, a heart, something essential. He uses contracts, literal legal agreements with supernatural enforcement. He finds loopholes and enforces them. He does not generally cheat — he manipulates the terms themselves. [game_w3]

## Relationships

- **Olgierd von Everec:** O'Dimm's most recent major contractor before the events of Hearts of Stone. Olgierd asked for immortality, success, and the heart of the woman he loved. O'Dimm gave him immortality and "hardened his heart" — literally made it stone — ensuring Olgierd could not love anyone, including the woman he wished to be with. The deal was technically fulfilled in the worst possible way. [game_w3]
- **Geralt:** O'Dimm uses Geralt as an instrument to discharge Olgierd's remaining debts (the three tasks). He is consistently polite and helpful to Geralt throughout. He is also using him completely. He has accounted for Geralt's capabilities and the possibility that Geralt might try to outwit him. [game_w3]
- **Shani:** Geralt's old friend from the books (a field medic who appears in Witcher 1 as well), who appears in Hearts of Stone. She helps Geralt navigate the Everec tasks. She has no particular connection to O'Dimm but her presence as a grounding, human-warmth element contrasts well with O'Dimm's cold precision. [game_w3]

## Velen and Novigrad Context

O'Dimm has no presence in the Velen or Novigrad arcs as an active character. The brief White Orchard appearance is the only main-game contact.

His deals and their aftereffects exist in the world — Olgierd von Everec is somewhere out there, immortal and heartless — but this context is only available retroactively after completing Hearts of Stone. [game_w3]

## DLC Content — Hearts of Stone

**The setup:**
Geralt is hired for a contract by Olgierd von Everec — a man who cannot be killed. During the contract (defending Olgierd's gang in the sewers), Geralt is attacked, and O'Dimm appears to "save" him, having arranged the situation to create a debt. Geralt must complete three tasks for Olgierd on O'Dimm's behalf, after which O'Dimm will collect Olgierd's soul.

**The three tasks:**
1. **The House of Borsodi:** Geralt must obtain the estate of Maximilian Borsodi. This involves a casino heist — the Borsodi family runs the House of Borsodi, a gambling establishment with a famous safe. The heist involves choosing between Vault and Auction paths, working with a crew, and navigating the Borsodi brothers' mutual antagonism.

2. **The Toad Prince:** "Get the caress of a princess, of the toad prince." Geralt enters a fairy tale reality — a wedding ceremony in a pocket dimension populated by the remains of a dead witch's constructs. A princess trapped with a toad-cursed nobleman. The task is a literal fairy tale Geralt has to play through.

3. **A Starry Night Above the Tower:** Geralt must enter Iris von Everec's house — Olgierd's dead wife — and retrieve something from the dream-reality she preserved in the mansion after her death. Iris's spirit is still present. The house is full of wraiths that are manifestations of her grief and loneliness. This is the DLC's finest piece of writing. Geralt experiences Iris's memories of Olgierd's decline — his increasing coldness, his inability to love once O'Dimm hardened his heart, the way he left her alone. Iris chose to stay in the house and preserve the memory of who Olgierd was, rather than accept the person he became.

**The final confrontation:**
With the three tasks done, O'Dimm moves to collect Olgierd's soul. Geralt can choose to let this happen (Olgierd dies, O'Dimm gets his prize) or attempt to free Olgierd using a riddle O'Dimm must acknowledge.

**The riddle:**
O'Dimm offers a riddle as the mechanism for Geralt to challenge the contract. The riddle is "What am I?" — what is O'Dimm? The answer must be something O'Dimm cannot refute, something that is true of him and that captures a limitation.

The answer is that O'Dimm is a reflection — a mirror — that shows others but has no image of itself. He casts no shadow. He is the Merchant of Mirrors who is himself not reflected. "Nothing" (no image, no shadow, no reflection of his own) is the core of the answer.

Correctly answering the riddle binds O'Dimm by his own rules. He must release Olgierd. He does so with controlled anger — the specific fury of something that has been outplayed by its own system — and leaves. He does not threaten revenge explicitly but the implication is clear. He has a long memory.

**If Geralt lets O'Dimm take Olgierd:**
O'Dimm collects the soul. Olgierd dies (finally — his immortality ends with the contract's resolution). Olgierd's death is arguably what he deserves for his original selfish bargain, but also what he's been wanting — he has been trapped in immortality and heartlessness for years and is not enjoying it. O'Dimm departs satisfied. Geralt is rewarded by O'Dimm with specific gifts.

**Olgierd's reward (if freed):**
Olgierd, freed from O'Dimm's contract, is mortal again. His heart is still stone — that change is not reversed — but he can live and die normally. His reward to Geralt is Iris's horse (a supernatural horse named Unicorn... no, the horse is named Roach replacement? Actually Olgierd gives Geralt his horse as a reward, which is a fine horse). The other potential reward from O'Dimm's concession is a wish — Geralt can ask for something. The available wishes and their effects vary. [game_w3]
