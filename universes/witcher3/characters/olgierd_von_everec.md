# Olgierd von Everec

## Overview

Olgierd von Everec is a Redanian nobleman, an immortal brigand, and the architect of his own destruction — the client whose debt to Gaunter O'Dimm sets Hearts of Stone in motion. He made a deal: he wanted to marry Iris (the woman he loved, whose family would not approve the match), he wanted to succeed, he wanted to be untouchable. O'Dimm gave him all of this through a deal that also made him immortal and turned his heart to stone — literally, as a condition of the contract, he cannot feel love or deep emotion. He got everything he asked for and lost the only thing that mattered: the ability to love Iris, who loved him regardless. He has spent years since becoming progressively worse — reckless, cruel, indifferent to the suffering he causes — because a man with a stone heart and no mortal consequence has very little to anchor him. He is not sympathetic in a simple way. He is sympathetic in the more uncomfortable way of watching someone who deserved better from himself than he chose.

## Key Facts

- **Full name:** Olgierd von Everec [game_w3]
- **Status:** Immortal (cannot be killed by conventional means) as a result of O'Dimm's deal [game_w3]
- **Condition:** Heart of stone — he cannot feel love or deep emotion. This is a literal magical state, not a metaphor. [game_w3]
- **Background:** Redanian minor nobility. The family's finances were troubled; he needed resources to court Iris Bilewicz, whose family considered him unsuitable. He made his deal with O'Dimm partly to solve these material problems and partly out of genuine, if reckless, love. [game_w3]
- **Brother:** Vlodimir von Everec — also dead (killed, not by Olgierd, but during the period of the gang's activities). He appears in Hearts of Stone as a ghost Geralt has to contend with. Vlodimir is chaos energy in spectral form. [game_w3]
- **Current activities:** Leads a gang of brigands. Not meaningfully organized toward anything. He is occupying his immortality. [game_w3]
- **The three tasks:** Before the Hearts of Stone contract structure, Olgierd had made a deal with O'Dimm that required specific tasks to be completed as part of the bargain's terms. These tasks (House of Borsodi, the Toad Prince, the night sky from the tower) are the debts he passes to Geralt. [game_w3]

## Relationships

- **Iris von Everec (deceased):** His wife. He loved her before his heart turned to stone. After his heart turned, he could no longer feel that love — he became cold and abandoning rather than attentive. She died alone in their mansion, preserving the memory of who he was before O'Dimm's deal changed him. His grief for what he lost — for who he was before — is the closest thing to feeling he can access. [game_w3]
- **Gaunter O'Dimm:** His contractor. O'Dimm technically fulfilled every term of the original deal. The deal was a trap. Olgierd knows this and cannot do anything about it. [game_w3]
- **Geralt:** Geralt is hired by Olgierd at the DLC's start for a monster contract, which O'Dimm has set up to create Geralt's obligation. Their interactions are businesslike with an edge of Olgierd's recklessness — he treats Geralt as another piece moving through O'Dimm's board without meaningful acknowledgment that Geralt is his own person. [game_w3]
- **Vlodimir von Everec (ghost):** His brother, who died during the same period as the gang's activities. Vlodimir is instructed (by Olgierd, through O'Dimm's task structure) to possess Geralt for a specific mission — attending a wedding, behaving normally. Vlodimir possessing Geralt, who is experiencing Vlodimir's personality overlaid on his own, is one of the DLC's more genuinely funny moments. [game_w3]

## DLC Content — Hearts of Stone

**The three tasks through Geralt's eyes:**

**The House of Borsodi:** Geralt assembles a crew for a heist on the Borsodi estate — specifically the vault that contains the house's deed. The Borsodi brothers (Horst and Ewald) have a violent rivalry; the vault itself contains their grandmother's will and both brothers' claims. The heist involves social infiltration, a daring vault entry, and a choice at the end: whose side to take, how to navigate the brothers' mutual hostility. [game_w3]

**The Toad Prince:** A fairy tale pocket reality. A princess bound by enchantment to a toad-creature (a prince cursed for cruelty). The task requires getting the princess's caress — her voluntary affection — as proof the condition is met. This involves navigating the fairy tale's internal logic, which includes an enchanted princess who may not be what she appears and a toad who may not be simply a monster. [game_w3]

**Iris von Everec's house — "Dead Man's Party":**
This is the DLC's finest chapter. Geralt must enter the Von Everec estate, where Iris's spirit is still present, and retrieve a painting (the stars above the tower) that Olgierd commissioned as a gift before he became what he is now.

The estate is a preserved pocket of memory — Iris's grief and love have kept the place in a kind of suspended state. The wraiths that populate the house are manifestations of her pain: four phantoms representing Loneliness, Sorrow, Misery, and Anger. To complete the task, Geralt must navigate a sequence of memory-scenes that reveal what Iris and Olgierd's marriage became.

The scenes show: Olgierd's increasing coldness and abandonment after the deal changed him. Iris's choice to stay and love him even when he could no longer receive it. Her death in the house, alone, with her paintings as the only company. The mansion is haunted not by Iris's ghost in the malevolent sense but by the perfect preservation of a love that had no one left to love.

When Geralt finally retrieves what he came for, Iris appears — she asks only that he leave her cat (a black cat that has somehow survived in the haunted house) in peace. The request is heartbreaking in its smallness. [game_w3]

**The ending choice:**

After the three tasks, O'Dimm moves to collect Olgierd's soul. Geralt has two options:

**Let O'Dimm collect:** Olgierd dies (finally — he has been in an immortal limbo). He accepts it. One reading is that the release from his stone heart and his pointless immortality is what he's been wanting. O'Dimm rewards Geralt generously. The moral accounting: Olgierd made a selfish deal, hurt many people during his immortality, and dies for it. [game_w3]

**Defeat O'Dimm's riddle and free Olgierd:** Geralt uses the riddle mechanism to bind O'Dimm by his own rules and free Olgierd from the contract. Olgierd is mortal again. His heart remains stone — that isn't reversed — but he can live and die. What he does with this freedom is left to him. His reward to Geralt is his white horse (a remarkable animal, fast and fine).

The question of whether Olgierd deserves to be freed is genuinely open. He was selfish originally. He became cruel in his immortality. He loved Iris once and lost that capacity. Whether the man who remains is worth saving is something each player (and Geralt) has to determine for themselves. The game does not answer it for you. [game_w3]
