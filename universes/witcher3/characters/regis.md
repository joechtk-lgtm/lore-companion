# Emiel Regis Rohellec Terzieff-Godefroy (Regis)

## Overview

Emiel Regis Rohellec Terzieff-Godefroy — called Regis — is a Higher Vampire, and among the most beloved characters in the Witcher book series. He traveled with Geralt's company during the books' central quest (the journey to rescue Ciri), was one of the most intellectually and morally sophisticated members of the group, and died at Stygga Castle in one of the saga's most painful moments. He is also alive in Blood and Wine, having regenerated over the intervening decades. His return is the most quietly miraculous thing that happens in any Witcher game.

## Key Facts

- **Full name:** Emiel Regis Rohellec Terzieff-Godefroy [books] [game_w3]
- **Species:** Higher Vampire — an entirely distinct species from humans that arrived via the Conjunction of the Spheres. Not undead, not related to the folkloric vampire other than superficially. [books] [game_w3]
- **Age:** Several centuries old. Higher Vampires are effectively immortal under normal circumstances. [books]
- **Appearance:** Slight, pale, with dark hair and eyes that occasionally show their vampire nature under stress. Typically presents as an unremarkable middle-aged man. [books]
- **Occupation in the books:** Barber-surgeon traveling the Continent. A quiet, skilled healer doing mundane useful work. [books]
- **Speech:** Formal, precise, with an old-fashioned courtesy that comes from actually being old. Vocabulary and phrasing of a different era. He is centuries old and it shows in how he talks. [books]
- **Reformed:** Regis swore off blood drinking — a vampire equivalent of alcoholism recovery. He maintained this commitment across the entirety of the books and the long years after. [books]
- **Death at Stygga Castle:** Destroyed by Vilgefortz's magic — immobilized by an iron rod through his body, then reduced to ash. Given centuries, a Higher Vampire can regenerate from such destruction. He did. It took decades. [books]

## Relationships

- **Geralt:** A genuine friendship from the books — they traveled together for months, through extremely difficult circumstances, and came to trust each other profoundly. Regis's intelligence and moral clarity complemented Geralt's pragmatism. Geralt's grief at his death was real and barely processed. Their reunion in Blood and Wine is one of the most emotionally earned moments in the games. [books] [game_w3]
- **Dandelion (Jaskier):** Fellow member of the traveling company in the books. Dandelion documented their journey; Regis was the subject of some of his more unusual observations. [books]
- **Milva:** Another companion from the books. Also deceased by Blood and Wine's timeline. [books]
- **Dettlaff van der Eretein:** A friend in the vampire community. Regis knew Dettlaff before the events of Blood and Wine and feels a degree of responsibility for what Dettlaff has become — specifically for not anticipating that Dettlaff's emotional volatility made him vulnerable to Syanna's manipulation. [game_w3]

## Velen and Novigrad Context

Regis does not appear in the main game. His absence is not felt directly unless the player knows the books — in which case his absence is conspicuous, because he was Geralt's companion and died, and his death was not undone publicly.

The events of Blood and Wine explain that Regis has been reconstructing himself for decades in relative isolation, away from the people he knew and the world he'd traveled. He found Geralt's reputation and eventually found Geralt specifically in Toussaint, drawn by both friendship and the situation with Dettlaff. [game_w3]

## DLC Content

**Blood and Wine:**
Regis appears at the DLC's start, finding Geralt in Toussaint. The reunion scene is short and controlled on both their parts — two people who have been through too much to make a fuss about things — and quietly devastating for anyone who read the books.

He is older, whole, and his sense of humor is intact. He assists Geralt in the investigation of the murders because he knows Dettlaff and can get Geralt access to him for negotiation rather than simply direct combat. Regis's knowledge of Higher Vampire psychology and culture is essential to understanding what Dettlaff is and why he's been manipulated into becoming the Beast of Beauclair.

**Regis and Dettlaff:** Regis is Dettlaff's friend and feels responsible for the situation. He wants Dettlaff to survive it if possible — he knows Dettlaff is a victim of manipulation (Syanna used his love for her to make him a weapon). He does not excuse the murders but he understands the mechanism and wants a resolution that doesn't end with killing his friend.

**The final confrontation options:**
Depending on how Geralt managed the investigation:
- If Geralt gets the ribbon from the fairy tale realm (Launeddas) and completes the preparation: Syanna can survive Dettlaff's attack. Dettlaff can be negotiated with or killed depending on Geralt's approach.
- Without the ribbon: the confrontation is more constrained.
- **Best ending for Regis:** Dettlaff is convinced to stand down (he leaves the Continent) or is killed if that was unavoidable, the Duchess is reconciled with Syanna, and Regis is not forced to watch his friend die or be the instrument of his death.
- **Worst outcome for Regis:** He has to help kill Dettlaff. He does it if he must. He doesn't celebrate.

**After Blood and Wine:**
In the best ending of Blood and Wine, Regis visits Geralt at Corvo Bianco. The scene is brief, warm, and correct — these are people who don't require grand statements. He's doing well. He'll be fine. He has plenty of time. He's a Higher Vampire. [game_w3]

## Books Canon (No Spoiler Tier — Background)

For users who haven't read the books: Regis's significance in Blood and Wine only fully lands if you know he died at Stygga Castle in "The Lady of the Lake" (the final Witcher novel). He was a genuine companion, one of Geralt's most trusted people, and his death was final by all appearances. His return in Blood and Wine is the games' best moment of honoring the books' emotional history. [books]
