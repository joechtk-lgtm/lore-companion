# The Nilfgaard Empire

## Overview

The Nilfgaard Empire is the dominant political and military power in the known world at the time of Witcher 3. Based in the far south — the continent's southern hemisphere is dominated by Nilfgaard proper and its vassal states — the Empire is ruled by Emhyr var Emreis, a cold and brilliant man who has waged two major northern campaigns and is currently winning the third. The Northern Kingdoms — Redania, Temeria, Aedirn, Kaedwen — are fragmented by internal politics, incompetent or tyrannical leadership, and the persistent inability to cooperate against a common threat. Nilfgaard's military is professional, disciplined, and extremely well-supplied. The Northern armies are brave and disorganized. The gap shows.

## Key Facts

- **Capital:** The city of Nilfgaard in the far south. [books] [game_w3]
- **Current Emperor:** Emhyr var Emreis, known as the White Flame Dancing on the Barrows of his Enemies — the most elaborate imperial epithet in common use on the Continent. [books] [game_w3]
- **Military:** Standing professional army organized on a legionary model. Disciplined, well-equipped, mobile. Black armor and black banners give them their Northern nickname: "the Black Ones." [game_w3] [books]
- **Vassal states:** Numerous. Including Toussaint (the duchy of Blood and Wine, culturally independent but formally Nilfgaardian), Nazair, Metinna, Ebbing, and others. [books] [game_w3]
- **Intelligence service:** The Imperial Secret Service, headed by effective agents embedded throughout the Northern Kingdoms. Emhyr's intelligence operation is superior to his enemies'. [books]
- **Religion:** Nilfgaard reveres the Great Sun, a solar deity. The Church of the Eternal Fire (Northern Kingdoms) is not Nilfgaardian; the two religious systems are distinct and partly opposed. [books]
- **Treatment of mages:** Unlike the Northern Kingdoms (where mages were integrated into the Brotherhood of Sorcerers), Nilfgaard's relationship with mages is more controlled — mages serve the Empire directly rather than through an independent organization. Fringilla Vigo represents the Lodge member who serves Nilfgaard. [books] [game_w3]
- **The Northern Wars:** Nilfgaard has fought three major campaigns in the Northern Kingdoms. The First Nilfgaard War ended badly for them (Battle of Sodden Hill, where mages held the line). The second campaign (which ended with Ciri escaping from Bremer var Emreis) was more ambiguous. The third — ongoing during Witcher 3 — has Nilfgaard winning decisively. [books] [game_w3]

## Relationships to Other Factions

- **Redania:** The primary remaining obstacle. Radovid V (Radovid the Stern) is a capable military leader despite increasing paranoia and cruelty. Redania's resistance is the only thing keeping the North from complete Nilfgaardian conquest. [game_w3]
- **Temeria:** Effectively destroyed. The kingdom of Foltest, who died in Witcher 2. Temerian resistance fighters (Vernon Roche, Ves) continue guerrilla operations under Nilfgaardian occupation. [game_w3] [game_w1w2]
- **The Lodge of Sorceresses:** Fringilla Vigo is the Lodge's Nilfgaard-affiliated member. The Lodge's relationship with Nilfgaard is complicated — they need to survive and Nilfgaard is the winning side, but individual members have Northern loyalties. [books] [game_w3]

## Velen and Novigrad Context

Velen — "No Man's Land" — is the occupied borderland that shows what Nilfgaardian conquest looks like on the ground. Nilfgaardian soldiers garrison the area from the ruined castle of the former Temerian lords. Their presence is normalized terror: soldiers with authority, no accountability, and no motivation to behave well.

The commanding officer in Velen is one of many local commanders who represent how the occupation works at the soldier level: orders from above, interpretation on the ground, and the understanding that conquered people have limited recourse.

In contrast to the pure-evil framing Northern propaganda applies to Nilfgaard, the Continent shows the grey: some Nilfgaardian officers are brutal, some are professional, the empire's administration after conquest is often more efficient than what it replaced. The peasants of Velen were squeezed by their Temerian lords too. The new overlords are foreign and their entry was violent, but the taxation and law that follows is not necessarily worse. This is not an endorsement; it is the game's refusal to simplify.

**The Garrison quest:** Geralt interacts with Nilfgaardian forces at multiple points in Velen, both in opposition and in pragmatic cooperation depending on player choices.

## Skellige and Beyond

Nilfgaard has attempted to influence Skellige's politics. The islands are difficult to conquer militarily (geography, naval tradition, warrior culture) but the succession crisis after King Bran's death creates an opportunity. Nilfgaard would prefer a jarl sympathetic to their interests — or at least one who will not actively campaign against them.

The succession choices Geralt makes on Skellige have secondary political implications for the Nilfgaard-North balance.

## Kaer Morhen Events

Nilfgaard is not directly involved in the Battle of Kaer Morhen. The Wild Hunt is the threat here, not Emhyr's legions. Nilfgaard and the Wild Hunt are separate antagonists with separate interests.

Emhyr var Emreis wants Ciri found and returned to him. The Wild Hunt wants Ciri for an entirely different purpose. Both are chasing the same person. This creates a structural tension in the narrative: neither faction is exactly Geralt's ally, and Geralt is working around both to protect Ciri on his own terms. [game_w3]

## Endings and Resolution

**If Radovid is assassinated:**
The Northern war's conclusion depends on whether the Temerian resistance (Roche, Ves, Thaler) or Dijkstra's Redanian faction moves into the power vacuum.

- **Dijkstra takes power:** He betrays the plan after Radovid is dead, tries to eliminate Geralt's allies (Roche and Ves), attempts to become the dominant Northern power himself. Nilfgaard wins the war. The Northern Kingdoms fall under Nilfgaardian control.
- **Roche/Ves/Thaler succeed:** The Temerian resistance leverages the chaos into a negotiated autonomy deal with Nilfgaard. Temeria becomes a vassal state with internal governance rights. It is a pragmatic compromise that saves Temerian identity at the cost of sovereignty. Nilfgaard nominally wins but Temeria survives.

**If Radovid lives:**
Radovid wins the war. The Northern Kingdoms "win," but under a man who is becoming a tyrant. He continues his anti-mage, anti-nonhuman policies with increasing savagery. The Northern victory is hollow.

**Ciri becoming Empress:**
If Ciri takes Nilfgaard's throne, the Empire itself changes. She is her father's heir but not her father — she has different values, different instincts, and the character to act on them. Whether she succeeds in reforming the empire from within is left open, but the suggestion is that she is the best possible version of what Nilfgaard could be. [game_w3]

**Emhyr's role:**
If Ciri becomes Empress, Emhyr does not oppose her. He cedes the throne willingly. This is not softness — it is the plan having succeeded. He always wanted Ciri to inherit. [game_w3]

## DLC Content

**Blood and Wine:**
Toussaint is formally a Nilfgaardian vassal duchy, though culturally independent to the point of seeming entirely separate. Duchess Anna Henrietta rules it with genuine authority. The Nilfgaardian connection is felt in the formal legal structure and in the fact that major crimes (like the serial murders) become matters involving Imperial oversight.

The contrast between Toussaint's fairy-tale surface (wine, tournaments, chivalric codes) and the darkness underneath (vampires, political schemes, the Duchess's hidden history with her sister) is Blood and Wine's central thematic tension. Nilfgaard as an empire is background; Toussaint as a place is the story. [game_w3]
