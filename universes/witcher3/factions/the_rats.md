# The Rats

## Overview

The Rats were a gang of young outlaws — teenagers and young adults, mostly — operating in the area of Nazair and surrounding southern territories during the period between Ciri's escape from Cintra and the book saga's conclusion. They robbed merchants, killed those who resisted, and lived outside the law by choice and necessity in roughly equal measure. Ciri fell in with them during her wandering years, took the name Falka among them, became their most effective fighter, and did things during this period that she carries as permanent guilt. The Rats are a books-only element — they are not referenced directly in Witcher 3 — but they are essential background for understanding who Ciri is, why she has specific guilt, and what she means when she talks about choices she cannot unmade.

## Key Facts

- **Period:** Between Ciri's escape from Cintra and the book saga's conclusion — approximately three to four years of her adolescence [books]
- **Territory:** Nazair, southern territories, borderlands [books]
- **Status:** Destroyed by Leo Bonhart (bounty hunter) [books]
- **Canon source:** Books only. Not referenced in the games. [books]
- **Ciri's name among them:** Falka — after the historical Falka, a Temerian princess who led a peasant revolt and was remembered as a monster and a tyrant. Ciri chose this name. The choice says something about how she saw herself during this period. [books]

## Members

- **Giselher** — The Rats' leader. A young man who assembled the group from outcasts and runaways. He led with force of personality more than formal authority — the Rats followed him because he was decisive and because alternatives were worse. [books]
- **Mistle** — Ciri's closest relationship in the Rats. A young woman, hard, from a difficult background that didn't allow softness. She and Ciri became lovers. Mistle was the first person after Kaer Morhen who genuinely chose Ciri's company rather than Ciri's destiny. She was also, by any objective measure, a criminal. Both things were true simultaneously. [books]
- **Kayleigh** — A member. Reckless, quick to violence. [books]
- **Reef** — A member, quieter than Kayleigh. [books]
- **Iskra** — A member. Female. [books]
- **Asse** — A member. [books]

## What Ciri Did With the Rats

Ciri was not a passive member. She fought. She killed. She robbed. She was the most physically capable member of the group by a significant margin — witcher training made her terrifyingly effective as a brigand — and she used that capability. She killed people who resisted the Rats' raids. She killed people who threatened the Rats. The line between justified killing and murder became progressively blurred.

She called herself Falka. The name of a historical tyrant. She was processing, badly, the gap between who she had been trained to be (Geralt's daughter, a person of destiny, someone who mattered) and who she was in practice (a fugitive with no home and no purpose, running from the Wild Hunt). Falka was simpler: no destiny, no obligation, no weight of Elder Blood. Just a name for a killer. [books]

## Leo Bonhart

The person who destroyed the Rats.

Leo Bonhart is a bounty hunter — a hired killer, not a witcher — who hunted the Rats on a commission. He is one of the most frightening human antagonists in the book series:

- He collects witcher medallions. He is good enough that he has killed witchers and taken their medallions as trophies. This alone establishes his capability.
- He is not impulsive or cruel in the random sense. He is precise, patient, and indifferent to his targets' humanity in a way that is more unsettling than sadism.
- He killed most of the Rats, captured Ciri alive (the commission specified her alive), and held her for an extended period.

**Ciri's captivity:** Bonhart held Ciri and — as part of his own arrangement with his employers — had her fight in a gladiatorial arena (or series of duels) against other fighters. She was forced to kill people in controlled combat conditions, which is a specific kind of horror: killing to survive, under someone else's control, stripped of the choice of whether to fight at all.

She eventually escaped. Bonhart pursued her for the remainder of the book saga.

**Bonhart's death:** Ciri kills Bonhart at Stygga Castle in the book's climax — the same battle where Regis dies and where Geralt's story concludes. She kills him in direct combat. After everything he did to her and took from her, she kills him herself. This is specifically important to her. [books]

## Psychological Legacy in Witcher 3

The Rats do not appear in the game but their legacy is in Ciri:

**The guilt:** The period with the Rats is the source of specific guilt that is different from the tragedy of Cintra or the trauma of the Wild Hunt. It is guilt for choices she made — killing she chose to do, a name she chose to take, a person she chose to become. The other tragedies happened to her. The Rats period is something she participated in willingly, even eagerly, for a time. [books]

**The name Falka:** When Ciri mentions choices that can't be unmade, or shows specific self-doubt about who she is and what she's capable of, this is the subtext. Falka is the name of the person she became when she stopped trying to be anyone good. [books]

**Mistle's death:** Ciri loved Mistle. Mistle was killed by Bonhart when the Rats were destroyed. Ciri's grief over this is real and compounded by guilt — she loved someone who was, by any external measure, a criminal and a killer. Both things were true. The grief doesn't care about the second thing. [books]

**Why this matters for player choices:** Choices in Witcher 3 that reinforce Ciri's confidence and sense of self-worth are compensating, in part, for the Rats period — for a time when Ciri had no confidence and no sense of self-worth and chose an identity built on violence and notoriety. Helping her be sure of herself is helping her move past Falka.
