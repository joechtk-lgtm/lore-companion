# The Temple Guard (Eternal Fire Witch Hunters)

## Overview

The Temple Guard — more commonly and colloquially referred to as witch hunters — are the enforcement arm of the Church of the Eternal Fire in Novigrad and the surrounding territories. They operate with the Church's religious authority and, effectively, Radovid V's political sanction. They arrest, torture, interrogate, and execute individuals accused of practicing magic, consorting with mages, harboring elves or other nonhumans, and anything else that falls under the ever-expanding definition of heresy against the Eternal Fire. They are not a formal military unit — they wear ecclesiastical livery, not Redanian armor — and this is partly what makes them more dangerous: they operate outside the ordinary chain of military accountability and answer primarily to their religious superiors, who answer to political expediency.

## Key Facts

- **Full name:** The Temple Guard of the Eternal Fire. Colloquially: witch hunters. [game_w3]
- **Authority:** Church of the Eternal Fire, with political backing from Radovid V's Redanian government [game_w3]
- **Jurisdiction:** Novigrad primarily; operating in surrounding territories under Redanian control [game_w3]
- **Primary targets:** Mages, individuals with magical abilities, Lodge of Sorceresses members, elves, dwarves, and other nonhumans suspected of anti-human activity, "demon worshippers" [game_w3]
- **Methods:** Arrest, imprisonment in the dungeons beneath the Temple of the Eternal Fire, interrogation (torture), public trial, execution by burning [game_w3]
- **Uniform:** Church livery — the symbol of the Eternal Fire (a stylized flame). Armed with standard weapons (swords, crossbows). [game_w3]
- **Notable individual:** Caleb Menge is the head of the Temple Guard during Witcher 3 — a true believer, genuinely convinced of the righteousness of what he does. He is more dangerous than a mere opportunist because he is not pretending. [game_w3]

## The Church of the Eternal Fire

The Church of the Eternal Fire is the dominant religious institution in Novigrad and increasingly in Redania. It worships a solar/fire deity — the Eternal Fire — as a force of civilization, order, and human primacy. Its theology frames:
- Magic as dangerous and corrupting, requiring control or elimination
- Elves and other elder races as threats to human civilization
- Humans as the rightful inheritors of the Continent (post-Conjunction)
- The Church itself as the institution that stands between civilization and chaos

The Church's theology is internally consistent and built on real fears. Magic is genuinely dangerous when uncontrolled. The elder races do harbor resentment toward humans (with historical justification). The Conjunction did bring chaos. The Church's response to these real concerns is to scapegoat, purge, and burn — which is where theology becomes instrument of persecution. [game_w3]

## Caleb Menge — Commander of the Temple Guard

Caleb Menge is the head of the Temple Guard and the most important witch hunter in the Novigrad storyline:

- He runs the interrogation and execution operations
- He is a true believer — not a corrupt opportunist using the position for personal gain, though the personal power is clearly comfortable
- He has information that Geralt needs in the Novigrad arc, which requires getting close to someone who should immediately have Geralt arrested
- The confrontation with Menge is one of the Novigrad arc's more tense moments: Geralt in an interrogation facility, working a plan that requires not simply fighting his way through everything [game_w3]

## Operations in Novigrad

The witch hunters in Novigrad operate a comprehensive system of persecution:

**Surveillance:** Informants throughout the city. The Church has built a network of people who report suspicious activity — magical practice, nonhuman associations, seditious speech against the Eternal Fire — in exchange for Church protection and small rewards.

**Arrest:** Public arrests are made to demonstrate Church power and create public fear. The spectacle of an arrest — a mage being seized in a market, the crowd watching — is part of the mechanism. The public show of authority reinforces the Church's reach.

**Interrogation facilities:** The dungeons beneath the Temple of the Eternal Fire are used for interrogation. The methods are standard torture. The goal is confession — both for juridical purposes (the Church wants admissions, not mere accusations) and to extract information about associates, locations, and networks.

**Public executions:** Burning at the stake in the main square. These are public events. The Church frames them as ritual purification — destroying the corrupt individual in the eternal fire, saving the city through the sacrifice. Crowds attend. Some out of belief. Some out of fear of appearing to not attend. [game_w3]

**The Dike:** The area near Novigrad's docks functions as a holding and processing area for nonhuman prisoners — elves, dwarves, halflings swept up in raids. The conditions are described in terms that make clear this is not a functional criminal justice system. [game_w3]

## Relationship with Redanian Governance

The witch hunters operate with Radovid's political sanction but are not formally Redanian soldiers. This creates a useful separation: Radovid can support the purges without being directly accountable for specific atrocities (he is the king, not the inquisitor). The Church carries the ecclesiastical and moral weight; Redania carries the political infrastructure.

For practical purposes on the ground, there is significant cooperation: Redanian soldiers assist when Church operations require military muscle; witch hunters investigate and accuse while soldiers enforce. The overlap is managed informally. [game_w3]

## Velen and Novigrad Context

The witch hunters' presence in Novigrad is inescapable during the game's Novigrad arc. They patrol in visible numbers. They conduct raids. The climate of fear they generate is the backdrop for every mage-adjacent interaction in the city.

**Direct encounters:**
- The encounter with Menge (quest "It Takes Three to Tango" or the associated Triss/Philippa questlines) puts Geralt in the position of needing information from the most dangerous man in Novigrad's Church apparatus
- Triss's evacuation quest "Now or Never" runs directly against the witch hunters — Geralt fights them in the sewers to get mages onto the escape route
- The interrogation of Dandelion's associates is conducted by witch hunters, which is part of why finding him was complicated
- Multiple secondary quests in Novigrad involve the effects of the persecution — individuals hiding, people who have been arrested, communities that have broken under the surveillance

**Novigrad's nonhuman district:**
The nonhuman population of Novigrad (elves mostly, some dwarves and halflings) lives in the Bits — a deteriorating district that has become increasingly a ghetto under Eternal Fire pressure. The guards of the Bits are not there for the residents' protection. [game_w3]

## Skellige and Beyond

The witch hunters do not operate on Skellige. Skellige has no equivalent religious institution — the Skellige tradition is old Norse-adjacent, tribal, honoring ancestors and the sea. The Church of the Eternal Fire has not established a foothold in the islands, and Skellige's jarl-based government would not accept it if it tried. This is part of why Skellige remains a functioning non-theocratic society when Novigrad has become an inquisition state. [game_w3]

## Kaer Morhen Events

The witch hunters are not involved in the Battle of Kaer Morhen. The Wild Hunt is the threat; the Church's persecution is a parallel crisis that operates on a different geographic and institutional level. The mages who came to Kaer Morhen did so partly because they had nowhere else safe to go — the witch hunters made Novigrad untenable, Radovid's territories hostile, and Kaer Morhen remote enough to be defensible.

## Endings and Resolution

**If Radovid is assassinated:**
With Radovid dead, the witch hunters lose their primary political backer. The Church of the Eternal Fire continues — religious institutions outlast individual rulers — but without Redanian state power behind the purges, the witch hunters' authority contracts significantly. Mages and nonhumans have more room. The persecution doesn't end immediately but it loses its teeth.

**If Radovid lives and wins the war:**
The witch hunters expand with Redanian territory. As Nilfgaard is pushed back (or fails to advance further), Radovid's anti-mage, anti-nonhuman campaign becomes the governing philosophy for a larger and larger area. The Eternal Fire's authority grows. More burnings, more arrests, more systematic persecution.

**Long-term:**
The Eternal Fire's power base in Novigrad is robust enough to survive short-term political changes. Whether it remains as extreme as it was under Radovid's backing depends on who governs Redania (or the post-war North) and whether any leader has both the ability and inclination to constrain the Church's enforcement arm. ## DLC Content

**Hearts of Stone:**
Hearts of Stone takes place partly in the Oxenfurt-adjacent region, which is under Redanian control. Witch hunters have some presence in the territory but are not a focus of the DLC plot, which concerns Gaunter O'Dimm and Olgierd von Everec. The DLC does not engage with the Church of the Eternal Fire's politics directly.

**Blood and Wine:**
Toussaint has no Eternal Fire presence. The duchy's religious landscape is different — Toussaint's culture is based on a knightly code of virtues, not solar fire theology. The Church of the Eternal Fire is a Northern Kingdoms institution and has not spread to Nilfgaard's southern vassal duchies. [game_w3]
