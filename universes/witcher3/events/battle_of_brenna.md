# The Battle of Brenna

## Overview

The Battle of Brenna is the decisive military engagement that ended the Second Nilfgaard War — the conflict in which Nilfgaard's second major northern campaign was ultimately turned back by a Northern coalition. It is one of the most significant military events in the Witcher saga's recent history, demonstrating both the Northern Kingdoms' capacity for unified resistance when the situation demands it and the enormous human cost of that resistance. It is also the battle most directly associated with Triss Merigold's defining character moment — her choice to use all of her healing magic on injured soldiers rather than herself, and to fight the remainder of the battle with a severe, untreated wound. She survived. The fourteen mages who died at Sodden Hill (First War) are commemorated on a hill; Brenna is a different kind of memorial.

## Key Facts

- **War:** Second Nilfgaard War [books]
- **Result:** Northern coalition victory. Nilfgaard's second campaign ended. [books]
- **Location:** Brenna, on the Temerian-Sodden border region [books]
- **Northern commanders:** Queen Meve of Lyria and Rivia (a significant figure — she personally fought in the battle); multiple Temerian, Redanian, and allied commanders. [books]
- **Nilfgaardian commander:** Menno Coehoorn, one of Emhyr's marshals. [books]
- **Mage participation:** Northern mages fought at Brenna as a unified force — one of the last times the Brotherhood organized for direct military action. [books]
- **Human cost:** Enormous. Brenna was not a clean victory. The Northern coalition won but the casualties were significant on both sides. [books]
- **Aftermath:** Nilfgaard was pushed back. A peace treaty was negotiated. The Northern Kingdoms retained their formal independence (for the time being). [books]
- **Canon source:** Books. Referenced in Witcher 3 as background history. [books] [game_w3]

## The Battle

**The military situation:** Nilfgaard's second campaign had been going badly for the North — the fall of various territories, the political fracturing of the Northern alliance, and Nilfgaard's superior military organization had given the Empire significant ground. Brenna was a critical engagement where the North managed to commit coordinated forces with unified command (rare) against Nilfgaard's advancing forces.

**Queen Meve's role:** Meve of Lyria and Rivia is one of the more remarkable figures in the Witcher saga's background — a queen who fought in armor, who kept her kingdom functional through both Northern Wars, and whose determination at Brenna was a significant factor in the Northern victory. Her story is told more fully in the Gwent game "Thronebreaker: The Witcher Tales," which covers the period before and during Brenna. For Witcher 3 purposes: she was a major Northern commander, she fought personally, and she is one of the reasons Brenna was a victory rather than another defeat. [books]

**Menno Coehoorn's defeat:** Coehoorn was captured (or killed — accounts differ by source) at Brenna. The loss of his direct command disrupted Nilfgaard's coordination and contributed to their defeat at the engagement level. [books]

**The mages at Brenna:** The Brotherhood of Sorcerers participated in the Brenna engagement — this is one of their last significant organized military actions before the Thanedd Coup fractured the Brotherhood entirely. The mages' firepower (literally — Igni-scale effects at full sorceress capacity, not witcher-Sign scale) was a meaningful factor in the battle's outcome. [books]

## Triss Merigold at Brenna

This is the story behind Triss's title "the Fourteenth of the Hill" — though the memorial hill referenced is Sodden Hill from the First War, Brenna is where Triss's specific character-defining moment occurred.

**What happened:** Triss was fighting at Brenna as one of the Brotherhood's mages. During the battle, she was wounded — a severe wound that would have required significant healing magic to address before she could continue fighting effectively. She chose not to heal herself. She used every drop of her healing magic on injured soldiers around her instead. She fought the rest of the battle wounded.

The wound was real and serious. She fought through it. The aftermath required significant recovery time.

**Why she did it:** The soldiers needed the healing more than she did — or more precisely, the battle needed them healed and functional more than it needed her to be at full capacity. The utilitarian calculation was clear; the personal cost was significant. She made the choice without extended deliberation, which is sometimes how character is most legibly demonstrated.

**The title "Fourteenth of the Hill":** The fourteen mages who died at Sodden Hill (First Northern War) are commemorated as martyrs of the North's magical defense. Triss's title references them — she is "the Fourteenth" in the sense of the one who was supposed to die (given her wound and the conditions of the battle) but didn't. The title is equal parts honor and dark humor. She uses it as the latter. [books] [game_w3]

## Significance for Witcher 3

Brenna's victory ended the Second War and produced the peace that held until the Third War (ongoing in Witcher 3). Its effects on Witcher 3's world:

**The temporary peace:** Brenna established a Northern-Nilfgaard border that held for roughly a decade before Emhyr's Third War began. This peace gave the Northern Kingdoms time to squander whatever political unity Brenna required, which they did efficiently. By Witcher 3, they are more fractured than before Brenna. [books] [game_w3]

**Triss's credibility:** Her record at Brenna (and Sodden Hill) is why she is taken seriously as a mage by people who know the history. The Underground mage evacuation she runs in Novigrad requires trust and credibility; her wartime record is part of what she has to draw on. [books] [game_w3]

**Meve's legacy:** In a world where almost every Northern ruler in Witcher 3 is either dead (Foltest), mad (Radovid), compromised, or absent, Meve's wartime record represents what Northern leadership could have been at its best. She is not a character in Witcher 3 but she is part of the history that context the current rulers' inadequacies. [books]

**Nilfgaard's lesson:** Emhyr's Third War incorporates what was learned from Brenna — don't engage in set-piece battles with unified Northern command. Undermine the unity first (through the Thanedd coup's aftereffects, through the political fragmentation the Northern Kingdoms did to themselves), then advance. The Third War's Nilfgaard doesn't fight Brenna again. [books] [game_w3]
