# The Blaviken Massacre

## Overview

The Blaviken incident — known in popular parlance as the Blaviken Massacre — is the event that gave Geralt of Rivia the epithet "Butcher of Blaviken" and serves as the defining moral case study of his philosophy of the Lesser Evil. It predates the games by decades. It is based on the short story "The Lesser Evil" from Andrzej Sapkowski's collection *The Last Wish*. By the time of Witcher 3, it is old history and older guilt — the kind of thing that surfaces when someone wants to discredit Geralt or when Geralt himself is alone and thinking.

## Key Facts

- **Source:** "The Lesser Evil" in *The Last Wish* by Andrzej Sapkowski [books]
- **Parties involved:** Geralt of Rivia, Renfri (a.k.a. Shrike), Stregobor (wizard), Renfri's gang of seven men [books]
- **Location:** The town of Blaviken, a small market town [books]
- **Result:** Geralt kills Renfri's seven men in the town square, then kills Renfri herself. Townspeople drive him out. The name "Butcher of Blaviken" spreads. [books]
- **Canonical interpretation:** An attempt to choose the Lesser Evil that failed to feel like anything but a massacre. [books]

## What Happened — The Full Account

Geralt arrived in Blaviken to sell a kikimora's head to a herbalist for payment. He encountered Stregobor, an elderly wizard who had taken refuge in the town in a magically concealed tower. Stregobor was hiding from Renfri, a woman who wanted him dead.

**Renfri's history:** Born under the Shrike's conjunction (a celestial alignment associated with cursed births in Stregobor's theory), she was stigmatized from childhood. Stregobor and others who held his views believed women born under the Shrike's conjunction were mutated toward evil. Renfri's stepfather sold her to a criminal when she was a child. She was raped. She survived. She escaped. She became a bandit and outlaw of increasing violence and effectiveness, building toward a single purpose: reaching Stregobor, who she blamed (not entirely unreasonably) for the theory that made her life what it became. She killed in pursuit of this goal. She was a victim who became a killer. Both things were true simultaneously.

**Stregobor's request:** He asked Geralt to kill Renfri first, to protect him. He even offered to pay well. Geralt refused. He would not kill on behalf of a theory about cursed births. The theory was Stregobor's obsession and his excuse; Geralt would not be its instrument.

**Renfri's request:** She found Geralt separately, away from Stregobor. She told him she would spare Stregobor and leave Blaviken if Geralt spent the night with her. He agreed. They spent the night together.

**The morning:** Renfri had already sent her seven men into the town square while she and Geralt were together. They were taking hostages — townspeople, including a little girl — to use as leverage to force Stregobor out of his concealment. The night with Geralt was cover for this. She manipulated him into lowering his guard while she maneuvered her men into position.

Geralt came into the square and found the situation in progress: her men holding townspeople at weapons-point, Renfri watching. He had a choice. He could walk away and let her complete her plan — extract Stregobor and kill him, probably with some hostage casualties in the process. He could try to negotiate again, having just failed negotiation badly. Or he could act.

He killed all seven of Renfri's men in the square.

Renfri fought him herself. She was capable — she had been living by the blade for years. He killed her too. She died in the square. Her last words to him were prophecy-like: "The girl in the forest..." — a reference to Ciri, decades before Geralt met her.

**The aftermath:** The townspeople came out of their homes and found bodies in their market square and a witcher standing over them. They did not know what led to this. They saw what it looked like. They drove Geralt out of Blaviken. The name "Butcher of Blaviken" was coined that day and spread from there.

## Why Geralt Carries This

The guilt is not simple:

**He was used.** Renfri exploited the night they spent together as tactical cover. His willingness to look for a third option — to believe negotiation was possible — was the opening she exploited. He thought he had reached a resolution. He was being played.

**He chose to act.** He could have not fought. He made the calculation that fighting was the lesser evil — that the hostage situation, if allowed to play out, would cost more lives. The calculation may have been correct. It did not produce a clean outcome.

**Renfri was not a villain in any simple sense.** She was a child who was sold, a woman who was broken, a person who rebuilt herself around a purpose that was also revenge and also grief. Stregobor's theory about cursed births was cruelty with a philosophical name. She had reasons. Geralt killed her knowing her reasons.

**The Lesser Evil theory failed its test.** Geralt's moral framework — choose the lesser evil when forced to choose — is the closest thing he has to an ethical code. Blaviken was his attempt to apply it. He chose to prevent the hostage-taking; the townspeople interpreted the result as slaughter. The lesser evil still looked like a massacre. He stopped believing unambiguously in the framework after Blaviken.

**He wanted a third option and couldn't find one.** Every step of the Blaviken situation involved Geralt looking for a solution that didn't involve killing anyone. He refused Stregobor's request, he negotiated with Renfri, he tried to believe the night together was a real resolution. There was no third option. He had to choose. He chose. And then lived with what it looked like.

## The Name "Butcher of Blaviken"

The name is accurate in the narrow factual sense: Geralt killed eight people in Blaviken's square (seven of Renfri's gang, then Renfri herself). It is inaccurate as a characterization because it implies he killed for its own sake, without cause, as a monster would.

He has not argued the name for a long time. The argument requires explaining everything, and explaining everything has never made a mob feel differently. The name is his, and he carries it. [books]

## Relevance in the Games

**Witcher 3 context:** The event is background history by Witcher 3 — decades old, a fact that characters may know as a data point about Geralt's reputation. It is not directly revisited in quests. Its significance is thematic: Blaviken is why Geralt is skeptical of his own moral reasoning, why he resists being pushed into moral certainties, and why he knows that choosing the lesser evil does not make you feel good about it. [game_w3]

**Witcher 1:** Characters in the first game are more likely to reference the event directly, since the game is set closer in time to it and Geralt's reputation is more recently shaped by it. [game_w1w2]

## Netflix Version
[canon_source: netflix]

The Netflix series adapts "The Lesser Evil" in season 1, episode 1 (predominantly) and episode 4. The core events are present: Renfri, Stregobor, the fight in the market square. The adaptation significantly expands Renfri's backstory, making her sympathetic in ways that are less explicit in the short story. The ending follows the same trajectory. For canonical purposes regarding the books and games, the short story is the definitive account.
