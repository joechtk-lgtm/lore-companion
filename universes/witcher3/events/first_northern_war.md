# The First Nilfgaard War (The First Northern War)

## Overview

The First Nilfgaard War — also called the First Northern War — was Nilfgaard's initial major military campaign against the Northern Kingdoms. It ended in the Battle of Sodden Hill, where a coalition of Northern mages held the line against Nilfgaard's advance at catastrophic personal cost, checking the empire's expansion for the first time. It is the war that established the pattern that defines Witcher 3's political landscape: Nilfgaard is the dominant military force; it can be stopped only through unified Northern resistance and magical intervention; the Northern Kingdoms cannot sustain either for long. The mages who died at Sodden Hill are commemorated. The political unity that made their sacrifice meaningful did not survive the peace.

## Key Facts

- **Dates:** Approximately 1263, ending with the Battle of Sodden Hill [books]
- **Scope:** Nilfgaard's forces swept through the Southern kingdoms (Cintra, parts of Aedirn and Lyria-Rivia) [books]
- **Ending battle:** The Battle of Sodden Hill — fourteen mages held a pass against Nilfgaard's forces [books]
- **Result:** Military stalemate at Sodden. Peace negotiated. Nilfgaard retains conquered southern territory but the advance north is stopped. [books]
- **Casualties of note:** Fourteen mages died at Sodden. They are commemorated as the Fourteen of the Hill. [books]
- **Triss Merigold's role:** She participated in Sodden Hill, was severely wounded, but survived. This is why she is called the Fourteenth — she survived when fourteen died, making her the one who went through without dying, the anomaly. [books]
- **The Conjunction with Ciri:** The Fall of Cintra occurred during this war. Ciri's escape from Cintra and her arrival in Geralt's care both happened during or immediately after this war's events. [books]

## The Battle of Sodden Hill

Sodden Hill is the Witcher universe's equivalent of a last-stand battle that became the founding myth of Northern magical service:

**The situation:** Nilfgaard had taken Cintra and was advancing north. The Northern kingdoms' armies were fragmented. The Brotherhood of Sorcerers debated — some advocating intervention, some arguing neutrality. A coalition of mages eventually chose to act.

**The stand:** Fourteen mages took position at Sodden Hill — a defensible chokepoint — and used everything they had to stop Nilfgaard's advance. The battle lasted long enough for Northern armies to regroup and push back. Fourteen mages died. Nilfgaard was halted.

**The survivors:** Several survivors, including Yennefer and Triss, became politically significant figures in the post-war period. Triss received the title "the Fourteenth of the Hill" specifically because she survived when the others died — she had used all of her healing magic on injured soldiers before the final assault and fought the rest of the battle with a severe, untreated wound rather than heal herself. She lived through it. The wound was eventually treated. She does not talk about this often. [books]

**Yennefer at Sodden:** Yennefer participated and was captured after the battle, held by Nilfgaard for a period. Her captivity and eventual release is part of the backstory that shapes her attitude toward Nilfgaard in Witcher 3 — she has no illusions about Emhyr's intentions. [books]

**Political aftermath:** The peace treaty after Sodden gave Nilfgaard their conquered southern territories (formally — they already held them) and stopped the active advance. The Brotherhood of Sorcerers was praised for the victory and then began the factional disputes that would eventually lead to the Thanedd Coup. The Northern kingdoms did not cooperate significantly after the peace, beginning the fragmentation that Nilfgaard's second and third campaigns would exploit. [books]

## Relevance to Witcher 3

The First Northern War is the backstory that explains:

**Why Nilfgaard's expansion is existentially threatening.** They came before. They were only barely stopped. Now they're back and winning.

**Why the Brotherhood of Sorcerers no longer functions.** Sodden's aftermath, and then the Thanedd Coup that followed the second war, destroyed the organizational structure that made coordinated mage action possible. The Lodge of Sorceresses is the remnant of what Sodden and Thanedd left.

**Why Triss Merigold is the person she is.** She fought the battle. She treated soldiers instead of herself. She survived when she might not have. The courage that makes her believable in Witcher 3's Novigrad arc comes from somewhere.

**Why Yennefer does not trust Nilfgaard.** She was captured after Sodden. She knows what Nilfgaard does with prisoners who are valuable. Working under Emhyr during Witcher 3 is purely pragmatic; she has no illusions.

**Why the "Fourteen of the Hill" title means something.** When Triss is called this, it is not merely a nickname — it is a specific reference to a specific battle that shaped the entire political landscape of the world Witcher 3 takes place in. [books] [game_w3]

## In Witcher 3's Context

The war's legacy is present in every conversation about the Nilfgaard-North conflict in the game. When NPCs in Novigrad discuss the war — and they do, obsessively, because Novigrad is a city where the outcome matters to every person in it — they are measuring the current situation against the memory of the last time Nilfgaard came and was (barely) stopped.

The Eternal Fire's authority in Novigrad is partly built on the First War's fear: Nilfgaard as an existential threat, the city as a bulwark, and the Church as the institution that provided moral certainty during the chaos. Whether the Eternal Fire actually helped during the war is less important than that it claimed credit afterward. [game_w3]

## The War's Resolution in Witcher 3

The Third Nilfgaard War — ongoing during Witcher 3 — ends differently than the First depending on player choices. Unlike the First War, where the mages of Sodden held a line, the Third War's mages are scattered, hunted, and no longer capable of organizing a Sodden Hill-style intervention. The political resolution therefore depends on assassination, intrigue, and the personal choices of Geralt and his allies rather than a military holding action.

The contrast between the First War's heroic mage stand and the Third War's messy political resolution is part of how the game depicts historical decline: the first time, there were institutions capable of heroism at scale. The third time, there are only individuals making choices in limited contexts, and the outcomes are proportionally messier. [game_w3]
