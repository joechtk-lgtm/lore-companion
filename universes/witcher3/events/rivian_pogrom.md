# The Rivian Pogrom

## Overview

The Rivian Pogrom is the event at the end of the book saga — the event that killed Geralt of Rivia. It happened in the town of Rivia during the conclusion of the Second Nilfgaard War, when ethnic tensions between humans and nonhumans erupted into organized violence. Geralt intervened to protect elves being attacked by a human mob. He was stabbed by a human with a pitchfork. Yennefer, trying to heal him, died. Ciri used her Elder Blood power to take them both somewhere else — implied to be a place outside the normal world, sometimes called Avalon in the books. This is the ending of Andrzej Sapkowski's novel "The Lady of the Lake." It is the canonical origin of Geralt's amnesia in Witcher 1, and the last fixed point of the book saga before the games begin.

## Key Facts

- **Date:** At the conclusion of the Second Nilfgaard War, approximately 1268 [books]
- **Location:** Rivia — a city on the border of Lyria and Rivia, the place from which Geralt took his adopted name [books]
- **Nature:** An anti-elven pogrom during the peace celebrations following the war's end [books]
- **Geralt's death:** Stabbed with a pitchfork (or halberd — the weapon is described with specific pedestrian brutality) by a human rioter. He died of the wounds. [books]
- **Yennefer's death:** She used all of her remaining magical energy trying to heal him. The attempt killed her. [books]
- **Ciri's intervention:** She used her Elder Blood power to take both of them somewhere — the books suggest an Avalon-like realm outside normal time. [books]
- **Witcher 1 connection:** Geralt returns with amnesia in Witcher 1, having lost his memories of the book events. Yennefer also survived, eventually. The exact mechanism of their return is left ambiguous. [books] [game_w1w2]
- **Canon source:** "The Lady of the Lake" (book). Referenced in games as backstory. [books] [game_w3]

## The Pogrom — What Happened

The Second Nilfgaard War ended. Rivia was a city on the border — the region that would later be the independent "Kingdom of Rivia" that never entirely was. Peace celebrations were happening.

Anti-nonhuman tension had been building throughout the war. Elves and other nonhumans who had sometimes collaborated with Nilfgaard (since the North wasn't much better to them) were being blamed for the invasion's successes. The peace didn't resolve this — it gave the mob an occasion to act without immediate fear of consequences.

In Rivia's streets, humans attacked elves. It was organized violence: the mob had targets, had weapons, moved deliberately. Individual elves who hadn't left the city were caught and killed.

**Geralt intervened.** This is important: he didn't have to be there. He could have stayed away. He chose to act — to stand between the mob and the people being attacked. He was trying to stop violence against people who had done nothing to deserve it. He made the choice he usually makes when the choice is between action and inaction and inaction means people die.

A human in the mob stabbed him. Not a named assassin, not a powerful enemy, not a monster with supernatural capability — a person with a farming implement who reached through the chaos and ended the greatest witcher alive with a mundane puncture wound. The specific brutality of this is intentional. The Butcher of Blaviken, the White Wolf, the man who fought through the Wild Hunt's forces and killed creatures that could level walls — killed with a pitchfork by an anonymous rioter.

**Yennefer's attempt:** She was present. She had just come through the war's conclusion and was not at her strongest. She used everything she had left trying to heal him. The magical expenditure killed her. She died doing what she could for the person she loved. [books]

## Ciri's Intervention

Ciri was present — she had been moving toward Geralt and Yennefer throughout the book saga's climax, and the Law of Surprise (the connection of destiny between them) drew her there.

She found them dying. She used her Elder Blood power in a way she had not deliberately used it before — not to open a portal between locations in this world, but to take them somewhere else entirely. Somewhere outside the normal rules of the world they were in.

The books describe the aftermath ambiguously: Ciri places them in a boat, there is water, there is going somewhere that is not quite anywhere. The word "Avalon" is used, referencing the Celtic Otherworld. It is not meant to be literal — it is the books' way of saying that Ciri's power took them to a place that existed outside the dying-and-staying-dead rules of the Continent.

**Sapkowski's interpretation:** Sapkowski has said in interviews that Geralt dies in the book. The "Avalon" ending is a fairy tale conclusion — Geralt is gone, and Ciri's love for him creates a myth of his transcendence rather than a literal survival. From the author's perspective, the games are a continuation of the story he ended.

**The games' interpretation:** In the game continuity, both Geralt and Yennefer survived the Rivian pogrom via Ciri's intervention. Geralt returned without his memories. Yennefer eventually recovered as well. By Witcher 1, Geralt is alive with amnesia. By Witcher 3, he has recovered most of his memories. [books] [game_w1w2] [game_w3]

## The Amnesia

Geralt's return from wherever Ciri took him — and Yennefer's eventual return — cost Geralt his memories of the book saga's events. This is not clearly explained as a mechanism. The working assumption in the games is that the transition through Ciri's power, or the healing process, or the time in the Avalon-adjacent space, did something to his memory that couldn't be undone cleanly.

In Witcher 1, he has no memory of Yennefer, Ciri, or the events of the books. By the end of Witcher 2, he has recovered significant memories. By Witcher 3, he is essentially operating with full memory — the amnesia as a gameplay mechanic is resolved, and the character's history with Yennefer and Ciri is the foundation of the story. ## Significance for Witcher 3

The Rivian Pogrom does not appear as a quest or direct reference in Witcher 3 but is the backbone of the game's emotional world:

**Why Geralt and Yennefer's relationship is complicated:** They died together (or nearly) and came back to something uncertain. The djinn binding, the decades of on-again-off-again, the question of whether the bond is magical compulsion — all of this accumulated against a background of having been at the edge of final loss together. [books] [game_w3]

**Why Geralt refuses to let Ciri face things alone:** He knows what Ciri did at the Rivian pogrom. She used everything she had to bring them back. He owes her, and he loves her, in proportions that don't require calculation. [books] [game_w3]

**The irony of Geralt's name:** He is called Geralt "of Rivia" — a name he made up, a place he wasn't from. He died in Rivia, protecting its nonhuman residents from the city that bore his false name. Destiny's dark humor is consistent throughout the Witcher saga. [books]
