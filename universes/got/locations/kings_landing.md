# King's Landing

## Overview

King's Landing is the capital of the Seven Kingdoms, seat of the Iron Throne, and the largest city in Westeros — with a population of roughly half a million people. It was founded by Aegon the Conqueror after his landing at the mouth of the Blackwater Rush, on the hill where he and his sisters made camp after the Conquest. The city grew up around that hill. Three hundred years later it is the political, commercial, and cultural center of the realm, and it stinks. Sewage infrastructure was never adequately built for the population it has. The poor quarter, Flea Bottom, is one of the more miserable places in the story. The city is everything the realm produces, concentrated and compressed — its wealth and its cruelty and its ambition and its squalor all layered on top of each other.

## Key Facts

- **Location:** At the mouth of the Blackwater Rush on Blackwater Bay, on the eastern coast of Westeros. [book_asoiaf]
- **The Red Keep:** The royal castle at the city's highest point. Contains the Small Council chambers, the throne room (with the Iron Throne), the royal apartments, the dungeons (the black cells are beneath everything), and an extensive network of secret passages built by Maegor the Cruel. [book_asoiaf]
- **The Sept of Baelor:** The great sept of the Faith of the Seven, in the city's center. The holiest site of the dominant religion of the south. [book_asoiaf]
- **Flea Bottom:** The poor quarter. Dense, dangerous, malodorous. Where characters go when they need to disappear or find people who don't ask questions. [book_asoiaf]
- **The Street of Steel:** Where the smiths work. Where Gendry worked before Yoren found him. [book_asoiaf]
- **The goldcloaks:** The City Watch of King's Landing, identified by their gold cloaks. Corrupt under Janos Slynt; reformed somewhat by Tyrion, who had Slynt sent to the Wall. [book_asoiaf]
- **The Iron Throne:** In the throne room of the Red Keep — a monstrosity of melted swords, impossible to sit on comfortably by design. See separate entry. [book_asoiaf]

## The Wildfire Caches

This is critical and underappreciated context: beneath King's Landing, placed there by King Aerys II Targaryen during his descent into paranoia, are enormous caches of wildfire — the alchemists' green fire that cannot be extinguished by water, burns through stone and metal, and explodes under pressure. Aerys intended to burn the city rather than let Robert take it. Jaime killed him before he could give the order.

The wildfire is still there. In the books, this is confirmed and treated as a significant threat. In the show, Cersei uses it to destroy the Sept of Baelor. [show_s5_8] The amount remaining beyond what was used is unknown. [book_asoiaf]

## The Siege

Stannis Baratheon's assault on King's Landing — the Battle of the Blackwater — was the story's most significant military engagement in the first half. Tyrion's wildfire plan burned Stannis's fleet. The Tyrell-Lannister relief force broke the siege. The city held. [book_asoiaf] [show_s1_4]

## Cersei's King's Landing

Under Cersei's governance (post-Tywin), King's Landing became increasingly fractious. The Faith Militant was empowered. The Faith arrested both Margaery Tyrell and Cersei herself. The city existed in a state of armed religious tension that Cersei then used to her advantage. [book_asoiaf] [show_s5_8]

[spoiler_tier: show_complete]
## The Burning

Daenerys burned King's Landing even after its bells rang surrender. The Red Keep collapsed. Cersei and Jaime were buried in the ruins. The wildfire caches likely detonated as part of the conflagration. [show_s5_8]

The city survived in some form — Bran's council met in what remained afterward. But the burning of King's Landing was the act that defined Daenerys's arc in the show and led directly to Jon's decision. [show_s5_8]
