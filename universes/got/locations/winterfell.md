# Winterfell

## Overview

Winterfell is the seat of House Stark, capital of the North, and one of the oldest continuously inhabited castles in Westeros. It was built eight thousand years ago — legend says by Bran the Builder, who also built the Wall. The castle is enormous by the standards of the story: a double ring of walls, a great keep within, towers and halls and stables and a library tower and the hot springs beneath it all. The springs heat the walls, which is why Winterfell's stone is warm to the touch even in deep winter. This thermal system is an engineering achievement that predates most Westerosi technology.

## Key Facts

- **Size:** Covers several acres, with inner and outer walls. The outer wall is half a mile in circumference. It is large enough to feel like a small town, with its own blacksmith, kennel, stables, and winter town outside the walls for smallfolk who shelter there during hard winters. [book_asoiaf]
- **The hot springs:** Natural thermal springs beneath the castle feed a network of pipes that heat the floors and walls. This is why the castle is livable in northern winters. [book_asoiaf]
- **The godswood:** Three acres of natural forest within the castle walls, ancient and untouched. At its center stands a heart tree — a weirwood with a face carved into it, white bark, red leaves, centuries old. This is where the Starks pray. The godswood is connected to the old magic of the north; greenseers can see through weirwood trees. [book_asoiaf]
- **The library tower:** One of the best libraries in the north. Maester Luwin's domain. [book_asoiaf]
- **The broken tower:** Where Bran was climbing when Jaime pushed him. It had been struck by lightning and left unrepaired. The ravens nested there. Bran went there often. [book_asoiaf]

## The Crypts

Beneath Winterfell lie the crypts of the Kings of Winter — every lord and king of the Stark line, seated in stone with iron swords across their laps. The swords are to keep the spirits from walking. The crypts are deeper and older than the castle above them, going down into the rock farther than seems natural. The older crypts, the most ancient sections, are collapsed and have not been entered in living memory.

Something is in the crypts. The narrative has been gesturing at this since the first book, through Bran's dreams and the placement of the swords and the depth of the structure. What specifically is there is as yet unrevealed in the books. [book_asoiaf]

In the show, the crypts served as shelter during the Battle of Winterfell, and the dead raised by the Night King included the long-buried Stark ancestors. The show used the crypts as a reversal: the place characters sheltered became a source of danger. [show_got_s5_8]

## Theon's Occupation and the Burning

Theon Greyjoy took Winterfell with a small force while Robb was in the south. He held it briefly, staged the executions of two miller's sons as Bran and Rickon, and was then captured when Ramsay Bolton's forces arrived under the guise of besieging the castle. Ramsay flayed Theon's men, burned Winterfell, and left.

Winterfell stood ruined for much of the story — its towers collapsed, its walls blackened. The winter town around it emptied. The Boltons moved their seat there eventually and used the Dreadfort's tools on its stones. [book_asoiaf] [show_got_s1_4]

## The Bolton Occupation

Roose Bolton, named Warden of the North after the Red Wedding, installed his bastard Ramsay at Winterfell. The castle was partially rebuilt. Ramsay used it as his seat, held "Arya" (Jeyne Poole in the books [book_asoiaf] / real Sansa in the show [show_got_s5_8]) there, and made it a place associated with everything House Stark was not. ## Reclaimed

Jon and Sansa retook Winterfell in the Battle of the Bastards, killing Ramsay. The Starks returned. After the Long Night and the White Walker threat, Sansa was crowned Queen in the North at Winterfell. The direwolf banner flew again. [show_got_s5_8]
