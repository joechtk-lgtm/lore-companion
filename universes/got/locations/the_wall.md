# The Wall

## Overview

The Wall is the most impressive single construction in Westeros — seven hundred feet of ice (and more than ice, though exactly what else is unclear), three hundred miles long, stretching from the Bay of Seals in the east to the Gorge in the west. It was built at the end of the Long Night, roughly eight thousand years ago, by Bran the Builder — the legendary founder of House Stark who also built Winterfell. Its purpose was to prevent what happened during the Long Night from happening again: to keep the Others and the dead south of a barrier the Others could not cross.

The magic in the Wall is as important as the ice. Coldhands (an undead creature in the books serving Bran) cannot cross the Wall. The Others have not crossed it in thousands of years. Something in the structure, beyond mere ice, keeps them out.

## Key Facts

- **Height:** Seven hundred feet at the tallest points. This is enormous — visibility from the top extends for miles. [book_asoiaf]
- **Length:** Three hundred miles from the Gorge to the Bay of Seals. [book_asoiaf]
- **Composition:** Primarily ice, but the Wall does not melt in summer as it should. The cold maintained by it is beyond natural. Bran the Builder is credited with the magical component, though the exact nature is unclear. [book_asoiaf]
- **Construction:** The Night's Watch maintains the Wall, adding ice to damaged sections, cutting steps for access. The elevators (cages lowered and raised by pulley systems) carry men and supplies to the top. [book_asoiaf]
- **Original castles:** Nineteen at founding. Eighteen now abandoned, their gates sealed with ice and stone. Three remain manned: Castle Black (center), the Shadow Tower (west), Eastwatch-by-the-Sea (east). [book_asoiaf]
- **The tunnels:** Beneath each castle, tunnels run through the Wall's base. They are sealed with massive gates on both sides — the outer gates latched from within, the inner from the south. The tunnels are how men and Wildlings can pass through the Wall at all. [book_asoiaf]

## Castle Black

The Night's Watch's primary garrison, roughly in the center of the Wall. The Lord Commander's seat. The training yard, the armory, the Great Hall, the cells, the maester's quarters, the kitchens. By the time of the story it houses fewer than three hundred men when nine thousand are needed. It is perpetually understaffed, undersupplied, and dismissed by the lords of the south as a prison for criminals, which it partly is. [book_asoiaf]

The Wall to the north from Castle Black's top: miles of forest, rivers, open land, and then the unknown. The Haunted Forest, so named by the Watch. The Fist of the First Men (a hill where the ranging was destroyed). Craster's Keep. And beyond that, the Lands of Always Winter — where the Others come from. [book_asoiaf]

## The Battle of Castle Black

Mance Rayder's assault — coordinated from both sides of the Wall simultaneously — was the largest military attack on the Wall in living memory. Jon Snow led the defense from the top, then from the gate. The Watch held through the night. Stannis arrived and ended the assault. [book_asoiaf] [show_got_s1_4]

## The Wall's Limits

The magic that makes the Wall effective is specific to the Others. It does not stop the cold, the storms, or ordinary men. The Watch's undermanning means most of the Wall is undefended — a determined human army could cross at any of the abandoned castles. And if the Wall's magical component is broken — if something with the power of the Others or associated with them violates the structure — the protection may fail. [book_asoiaf]

## The Fall

The Night King rode Viserion — Daenerys's dragon, killed beyond the Wall and reanimated as an ice dragon — and destroyed the Wall at Eastwatch-by-the-Sea. The army of the dead walked south. [show_got_s5_8]

In the books, the Wall still stands at the end of published material. Whether this specific event — or something equivalent — happens in the books is unknown. The Wall's fall is show canon only as of the current published state of the series.
