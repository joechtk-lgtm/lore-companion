# The Free Folk (Wildlings)

## Overview

South of the Wall, they are called "Wildlings" — a word carrying contempt, implying savagery and lawlessness. North of the Wall, they call themselves the Free Folk. The distinction matters to them enormously. They do not kneel. They have no lords, no castles, no feudal system. A man's worth is what he can do; a woman's worth is the same. If you take a woman, she can take you back. If you can't hold her, you don't deserve her. This is not a joke to them. Their social system is built on direct demonstration of capacity, not inherited title.

They are fleeing south because they know what is coming. They have always known. They live in the same land as the Others. They have been watching them for generations.

## Key Facts

- **Name:** The Free Folk — never "Wildlings" among themselves. [book_asoiaf]
- **Population:** Hundreds of thousands scattered across the lands north of the Wall, in disparate tribes and groups with different cultures. Some are settled (the Thenns, a more organized group with armor and discipline). Some are nomadic. The giants are real — enormous humanoids who live among the Free Folk and ride mammoths. [book_asoiaf]
- **Their relationship with the Watch:** Generations of raiding southward, stealing livestock and people, have made them enemies of the Watch. The Watch has reciprocated. Neither side has been entirely wrong about the other. [book_asoiaf]
- **Their knowledge of the Others:** They know about the White Walkers. They have always known. The Night's Watch forgot what the Wall was built to stop; the Free Folk have not forgotten because they cannot afford to. [book_asoiaf]
- **Giants:** A real species among the Free Folk — enormous, few in number, riding mammoths. Several appear during the Wall assault in the show. Wun Wun, the last giant, dies at Winterfell in the show [show_s5_8]. [book_asoiaf] [show_s5_8]

## Mance Rayder

Mance Rayder was a former Night's Watch brother who deserted and went over the Wall. He united the Free Folk — dozens of disparate, historically hostile tribes — into a single coalition through sheer force of persuasion. This is the extraordinary achievement of his life: getting groups who had been enemies for generations to march together because the alternative was dying separately. [book_asoiaf]

He led the assault on the Wall because the Others were massing. He needed to get his people south, behind the Wall, where the magic that built it might offer some protection. He failed — Stannis arrived and broke the assault. He was captured. [book_asoiaf] [show_s1_4]

In the books, Melisandre burned a glamoured substitute (a Wildling Lord named Rattleshirt) in Mance's place and sent the real Mance on a mission to Winterfell disguised as a bard. In the show, Mance is burned (with Jon's mercy killing via arrow), and this plot is absent or given to different characters. [book_asoiaf vs show_s5_8]

## Ygritte

Jon Snow's partner among the Wildlings, a spearwife — skilled with a bow. She was the one who caught him during his ranging infiltration, and she was the one who pushed the relationship that followed. "You know nothing, Jon Snow." She meant it initially as an insult, about his ignorance of real life north of the Wall. It became something else. She was killed during the Battle of Castle Black by someone else's arrow. [book_asoiaf] [show_s1_4]

## The Battle and the Aftermath

The combined Wildling assault on the Wall — Mance's main force from the north, Tormund's smaller group from the south through the abandoned gate — nearly destroyed Castle Black. Jon Snow led the defense. Stannis's cavalry broke Mance's army from behind.

Jon negotiated with Mance and ultimately convinced Stannis to let the Wildlings survive as defeated enemies rather than be massacred.

[spoiler_tier: dance_of_dragons]
## The Great Migration

Jon's decision as Lord Commander to allow the surviving Free Folk through the Wall — to settle the abandoned castles and lands south of it, to fight the Others alongside the Watch when the time came — was the most controversial act of his command. The Watch brothers viewed it as a betrayal of everything the Wall stood for. The Wildlings viewed it as charity they resented needing. Tormund Giantsbane served as the main liaison. Jon's assassination followed shortly after. [book_asoiaf] [show_s5_8]

In the show, Tormund and the Free Folk survived the Long Night and the White Walker threat. Tormund walked north with Jon beyond the Wall at the story's end. [show_s5_8]
