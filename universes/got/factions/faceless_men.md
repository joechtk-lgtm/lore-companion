# The Faceless Men of Braavos

## Overview

The Faceless Men are the most skilled assassins in the known world and, according to their own theology, not assassins at all. They are servants of the Many-Faced God — death itself, the one god beneath all the names: the Stranger, the Drowned God, the Lion of Night, the Pale Child, the God of Death. All gods are the same god. All death is the same death. When the Faceless Men kill, they are delivering what they call "the gift" — a mercy, not a murder. This philosophy is either genuine spiritual insight or a very efficient justification for professional killing. The two are not mutually exclusive.

## Key Facts

- **Origin:** The Faceless Men began in the slave mines of Valyria. A slave prayed to his gods for death. No god answered. He prayed to all the gods. Death came — first to him, then to the suffering around him. He became the first Faceless Man. He eventually helped bring down Valyria, in the Doom. The founding story is told in the House of Black and White to those being trained. [book_asoiaf]
- **The House of Black and White:** Their temple in Braavos — open to all who seek the gift. No one is turned away, though the gift has a price: an equivalent sacrifice. Located in the city, accessible by boat. Black on one side, white on the other. [book_asoiaf]
- **Their ability:** They wear faces — literally, they can remove the faces of the dead and wear them, becoming that person in appearance. The faces are kept on the walls of a lower chamber in the House of Black and White. The mechanism is magical. [book_asoiaf]
- **The price:** The cost of hiring a Faceless Man is always high — equal to or greater than the value of the target. The Iron Bank of Braavos is sometimes said to use them for debt collection. Lords and kings can afford them; ordinary people cannot. [book_asoiaf]
- **Anonymity:** The Faceless Men have no names as individuals. They are "no one." The man Arya knew as Jaqen H'ghar has used other faces before and after; "Jaqen" was simply one identity among many. [book_asoiaf]

## Philosophy

The core teaching is selflessness to the point of identity dissolution. A Faceless Man has no self — no desires, no name, no history. They are servants of the god, and the god does not care who they are. This is why training someone is so difficult: a person who has lived a life of attachment, grief, love, and revenge (such as Arya Stark) has an identity that does not dissolve easily. She kept Needle. She answered to her name. She could not become no one, because she was still someone. [book_asoiaf]

## Arya in Braavos

Arya arrived in Braavos with the iron coin Jaqen had given her. The coin and the words "Valar Morghulis" were enough to get her access to the House of Black and White. She was taken in as a servant and then a student. The training involved cleaning bodies of the dead, learning to lie without tells (the House of Black and White has magical truth-detection — the kindly man could always identify falsehoods), and eventually learning to wear faces. ## The Price of Arya's Training

She was given assignments. She killed her first target. She killed a second. She killed Meryn Trant (show [show_got_s5_8]) / Raff the Sweetling (books [book_asoiaf]) — a personal kill, off-assignment, off the Many-Faced God's list. She was blinded as punishment.

She eventually recovered her sight by demonstrating she could use her other senses. She was given a final test — told to kill an actor playing Joffrey in a play. She discovered the true target was meant to be someone else entirely. She chose her own path, retrieved Needle from its hiding place, and killed the Faceless Man sent to kill her. She declared herself Arya Stark of Winterfell, not "no one."

The Faceless Men let her go. This either means she passed a test of identity, or that they had other uses for her. Or both. [book_asoiaf for the broad strokes; show_s5_8 for specific execution which differs in detail]
