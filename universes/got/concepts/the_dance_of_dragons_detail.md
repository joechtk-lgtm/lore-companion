# The Dance of the Dragons — In Detail

## What It Was

The Dance of the Dragons was a Targaryen civil war fought from 129 AC to 131 AC — roughly 170 years before the events of A Song of Ice and Fire. It was a war of succession between two half-siblings: Rhaenyra Targaryen (Viserys I's named heir, daughter of his first marriage) and Aegon II Targaryen (Viserys I's eldest son, born of his second marriage to Alicent Hightower). Both had legitimate claims depending on which reading of inheritance law one preferred. Both had dragons. The realm split. [book_fire_blood]

The Dance's deeper significance is not the succession question it answered (Rhaenyra's bloodline won; her son Aegon III took the throne) but what it destroyed: the dragons. [book_fire_blood] [show_hotd_s1_2]

## The Source Problem

Martin's *Fire & Blood* presents the Dance as history compiled by Grand Maester Munkun — drawing on accounts from Septon Eustace (a partisan of the Greens), Mushroom (the court fool, unreliable and bawdy but sometimes accurate), and others. The sources contradict each other. The motivations Martin assigns to characters depend on which account you trust. This is deliberate: the book is about how history gets written, and how the same events produce different narratives depending on who survives to tell them. The show *House of the Dragon* necessarily takes positions the book keeps ambiguous. [book_fire_blood] [show_hotd_s1_2]

## Timeline of Key Events

**The Coronation (129 AC):** Viserys I dies. Before Rhaenyra can be informed, Alicent Hightower and the small council crown Aegon II. The Dance begins with an act of interpretation — what Viserys intended and what was done with that intention.

**Rhaenyra's Mobilization:** Rhaenyra, at Dragonstone, learns of the coronation and begins assembling her forces. The Blacks' primary advantages: more dragonriders, the Velaryon fleet, and the oaths sworn to Rhaenyra by the realm's lords when Viserys named her heir.

**Rook's Rest:** Aegon II and Sunfyre fight Rhaenyra's ally Princess Rhaenys on her dragon Meleys (the Red Queen). Meleys is killed. Sunfyre is badly burned. Aegon II is severely wounded and spends months incapacitated.

**Blood and Cheese:** Aemond kills Lucerys Velaryon (Rhaenyra's son) above Shipbreaker Bay during what Aemond claimed was an unintended escalation. Daemon hires assassins who kill Aegon II's young son Jaehaerys in the nursery. The war becomes absolute.

**The Battle of the Gullet:** The Triarchy fleet is destroyed. Jacaerys Velaryon dies. The Blacks win the battle and lose an irreplaceable dragonrider.

**Rhaenyra Takes King's Landing:** The capital falls to the Blacks. Rhaenyra sits the throne. The city has already been bled by war and begins to starve.

**The Fall of Rhaenyra:** The city rises against her. She loses her dragon Syrax. She flees. She is captured.

**Rhaenyra's Execution:** Fed to Sunfyre in the dragonpit. Her son Aegon the Younger watches.

**End of the War (131 AC):** Aegon II is poisoned. Aegon III, Rhaenyra's son, is crowned. The Regency period begins — he is a child, and the realm is governed by regents for years.

## The Dragons That Died

This is the war's true cost. At its start: seventeen living Targaryen dragons. At its end: four. Within a generation of Aegon III's reign, none. The dragons born in the aftermath were smaller, weaker, increasingly sickly — the last died during Aegon III's reign. He is called "the Dragonbane" for it, though he did not intentionally kill them; they simply died and he did not mourn. He had watched his mother fed to one.

The dragons that died in the Dance:
- **Vhagar** — the oldest, largest, most formidable. Killed by Caraxes above the Gods Eye, sank into the lake with Aemond. She was over 180 years old.
- **Caraxes** — Daemon's dragon. Killed Vhagar but mortally wounded; crawled to shore and died there.
- **Syrax** — Rhaenyra's dragon. Released into King's Landing during the riots, killed by the smallfolk.
- **Meleys** — the Red Queen, Rhaenys Targaryen's dragon. Killed at Rook's Rest.
- **Meraxes** (note: already dead from Dorne) — not applicable, but context: the great dragons from the Conquest were already long gone.
- Several others, including Vermithor, Silverwing, Tessarion, and others — killed in the Battle of the Gullet, the Fishfeed, or in other engagements.

## What House of the Dragon Shows

The show adapts the Dance with significant creative interpretations. The show's Alicent is given more complexity and sympathy than the books allow — her friendship with Rhaenyra is a show invention that gives their conflict personal weight beyond political calculation. Daemon's relationship with Rhaenyra is romanticized. Aegon II is given genuine reluctance about his coronation that the books do not emphasize.

The show takes positions on questions the books leave ambiguous. It makes Alicent the moral center of the Greens rather than Otto. It presents Rhaenyra as clearly the legitimate heir and the Greens as usurpers rather than maintaining the ambiguity of the books. These choices define which story the show is telling. ## Why It Matters to the Main Story

Daenerys's dragons are the first in over a century. The Dance explains why they are unprecedented — the Targaryen dynasty had dragons for generations, used them to hold the realm, and then destroyed them in a family argument. The dragons' absence shaped all of Westerosi history from 131 AC onward. The great lords built power structures they would never have attempted when Targaryens had dragons overhead. The Iron Throne became something that could be contested by ordinary political means.

When Daenerys arrives with three dragons, she is not just arriving with weapons. She is arriving with the thing that made the Targaryen dynasty possible — the thing the Dance destroyed — and the world has forgotten what to do with that. [book_asoiaf] [book_fire_blood] [show_got_s5_8]
