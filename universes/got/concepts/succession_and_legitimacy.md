# Succession and Legitimacy

## Overview

The War of the Five Kings begins because of a succession dispute, and nearly every major conflict in the story has succession at its root. Westeros uses a modified agnatic primogeniture system — eldest male child inherits, with female children inheriting if no male heirs exist. The "modification" is contested: the first Dance of the Dragons was fought over whether a king's named female heir (Rhaenyra) outranked his male heirs from a second wife (Aegon II). The realm split. No definitive answer emerged from the chaos of civil war.

The question the story keeps asking is: what makes a ruler legitimate? And its answer is consistently: nothing, absolutely, which is why everyone keeps fighting.

## How Succession Works in Westeros

**Primogeniture:** Eldest child inherits. Male children are preferred over female in most great houses, though this is custom rather than absolute law and has been contested. [book_asoiaf]

**The Iron Throne specifically:** The Seven Kingdoms are theoretically a unified realm under one monarch. The throne passes to the king's eldest legitimate son. If no sons, to daughters. If no children, to the next of kin in the male line. This is the theory. Practice involves wars. [book_asoiaf]

**Legitimacy:** Born of a lawfully wedded couple. Bastards cannot inherit unless legitimized by royal decree (which the small council and king can do). Joffrey's illegitimacy — as Cersei's children by Jaime, not Robert's — is the specific legal fact that triggers the war. [book_asoiaf]

**Conquest:** Aegon the Conqueror created the Seven Kingdoms through conquest. This creates a logical problem: if conquest can create a claim, it can also overturn one. Any powerful enough claimant can argue that force validates itself. [book_asoiaf]

## The Specific Crisis

Jon Arryn discovered that Cersei's children were not Robert's. He was killed before acting on it. Ned Stark discovered the same. Stannis Baratheon independently discovered it and wrote to every lord in the realm. By Westerosi succession law, Robert's eldest legitimate heir is his eldest legitimate relative — which is Stannis, his brother. Joffrey has no claim. Tommen has no claim. Renly has less claim than Stannis even setting aside Joffrey's illegitimacy (as Robert's younger brother). [book_asoiaf]

The realm, collectively, chose Joffrey — the convenient fiction over the difficult truth. The law was clear. The lords chose power over law. This is the story's foundational statement about how governance actually works. [book_asoiaf]

## Five Kings, Five Claims

- **Joffrey Baratheon (Lannister):** Illegitimate by birth, king by possession of King's Landing and Lannister force. [book_asoiaf]
- **Stannis Baratheon:** Legitimate heir by law. No charisma, no support, entirely correct. [book_asoiaf]
- **Renly Baratheon:** Robert's younger brother, therefore behind Stannis in succession. King by popular support and Tyrell alliance. Charismatic. No legal basis. [book_asoiaf]
- **Robb Stark:** Not claiming the Iron Throne — claiming independence for the North and Riverlands. King in the North, by the declaration of his lords. Not entirely unlike Aegon's original claim. [book_asoiaf]
- **Balon Greyjoy:** Declaring independence for the Iron Islands. The weakest claim, the most self-serving declaration. [book_asoiaf]

## Jon Snow's Claim and the Succession Problem

Jon Snow is Aegon Targaryen, the son of a secretly married Rhaegar Targaryen and Lyanna Stark. If the marriage is legitimate — and there is evidence in both books and show that it was — then Jon is the legitimate heir of the Targaryen dynasty, senior to Daenerys (who is Rhaegar's sister, not his child). [book_asoiaf theories, show_s5_8 for confirmation]

This creates a succession crisis within the Targaryen "restoration": Daenerys is the claimant who did the work, built the army, crossed the sea, fought the wars. Jon is the person the law says should rule. The tension between earned claim (conquest, will, capacity) and inherited claim (blood, law) is exactly the tension the whole story has been tracing.

The show resolves this by having Jon kill Daenerys rather than rule. The books have not reached this point. [show_s5_8]

## The Dance of the Dragons as Precedent

The civil war between Rhaenyra's Blacks and Aegon II's Greens (see events/the_dance_of_dragons.md) was the previous Targaryen succession crisis, roughly 170 years before the main story. Its lesson: when two legitimate-ish claimants contest succession with dragons, the dragons die and the realm suffers for a generation. [book_fire_blood]

This is the historical context hanging over every succession argument in the main story.
