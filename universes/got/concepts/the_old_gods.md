# The Old Gods of the Forest

## Overview

The old gods are the faith of the north — and, before the Andal invasion six thousand years ago, the faith of all of Westeros. They have no names. They have no clergy. They have no temples. They have no texts. A follower of the old gods prays alone in the godswood, before the heart tree, without ceremony or intermediary. This is not simplicity — it is the deliberate absence of the institutional apparatus that corrupts other faiths. Whether the old gods are conscious, whether they hear prayers, whether they are in some sense the weirwood network itself — these are questions the books are circling.

## Key Facts

- **Weirwood trees:** White bark, red sap, red leaves, and faces carved into the trunk. The faces were carved by the children of the forest. They are everywhere the old gods are worshipped — and they are connected. The weirwood network is a kind of distributed consciousness or memory, through which greenseers can see anything that has ever happened near any weirwood tree. [book_asoiaf]
- **Heart trees:** Every godswood has a heart tree — the weirwood at the center, with the carved face, which is the focus of worship. Old and new castles throughout the north, and in some southern holdouts, have godswoods. King's Landing's Red Keep has a small godswood, though the tree there is an ancient oak — no weirwood. [book_asoiaf]
- **No priests, no dogma:** The old gods do not have organized worship. The sept has its septons and septas; the Red Faith has its Red Priests; the old gods have nothing of the kind. A man prays in the godswood alone. What he receives, if anything, is not institutionally mediated. [book_asoiaf]
- **The Andal invasion:** The Andals brought the Faith of the Seven from Essos six thousand years ago. They swept through the south, cutting down godswoods and building septs on the same sites. The north resisted — the Starks defeated Andal incursions and kept the old faith. The north is the only place where it survived in force. [book_asoiaf]

## The Children of the Forest

The children of the forest are the original inhabitants of Westeros — small, large-eyed, non-human creatures who lived in the forests for thousands of years before the First Men arrived. They carved the faces into the weirwood trees. The faces are not merely decorative; they are the nodes of a network of observation and memory. The children and the First Men fought, made a pact, and the children largely withdrew.

By the time of the main story, the children are believed extinct. Bran Stark finds them alive beyond the Wall, beneath the great weirwood tree where the three-eyed raven sits. They have been diminishing since the Long Night. A few remain, bound to the oldest trees. [book_asoiaf] [show_s5_8]

## Greensight and Warging

These are the supernatural gifts associated with the old gods' blood — the talents of those with First Men descent and strong connections to the weirwood network.

**Warging (skinchanging):** The ability to enter the consciousness of an animal — see through its eyes, control its body, experience its senses. Bran is an extraordinarily powerful warg; he can enter Summer, Hodor, and potentially other animals. Jon Snow has this ability with Ghost but uses it mostly unconsciously. Arya wargs Nymeria in her sleep in the books. [book_asoiaf]

**Greensight:** The ability to see the past, present, or future in dreams or visions. Bran's ability, massively amplified through training with the three-eyed raven. The greenseer can see anything that has ever occurred near a weirwood tree — which means nearly any event in the history of Westeros, since weirwoods were once everywhere. They can potentially also see forward, or influence events in the past, which raises questions about causality. [book_asoiaf]

The Hodor incident — Bran being connected through a weirwood vision to young Wylis in the past while simultaneously warged into present Hodor — demonstrated that the weirwood network allows some interaction across time. Whether this means the greenseer can change the past is one of the books' largest unresolved questions. [show_s5_8 for the specific execution of this scene]

## The Weirwood Network as Collective Memory

In the books, the weirwood network appears to be a vast distributed record of everything that has ever happened near a weirwood. The three-eyed raven has been embedded in it for so long that he has become part of it — he knows things that happened centuries ago as clearly as yesterday. He is transferring this access to Bran.

The old gods may be the network itself — a kind of presence that is not conscious in the human sense but is deeply aware, that hears prayers because the trees hear everything, that responds not through divine intervention but through what it knows and can channel. This interpretation is not confirmed in the books. It is strongly suggested. [book_asoiaf]
