# The Long Night

## Overview

The Long Night is the founding catastrophe of Westerosi civilization — an event eight thousand years in the past, before written records, preserved only in legend and the physical evidence of the Wall itself. It was a winter that lasted a generation, during which the Others emerged from the far north and swept south with an army of the dead, killing everything in their path. Civilization nearly ended. The Long Night and the War for the Dawn that followed shaped every culture in the world — the Night's Watch, the Wall, the obsidian weapons, the stories told in every tongue about a great darkness.

## Key Facts

- **Duration:** A generation — decades of continuous winter, no summer, crops failing, civilizations collapsing. [book_asoiaf]
- **The Others:** They came with the Long Night or during it. Whether they caused the cold or merely traveled with it is unclear in the books. The relationship between the Others and the long winter is not mechanically explained in the text. [book_asoiaf]
- **The wights:** The Others raised their dead, adding them to their army as they advanced south. [book_asoiaf]
- **The Wall:** Built at the end of the Long Night by Bran the Builder. Physical monument to the victory. The magic in the Wall keeps the Others at bay. [book_asoiaf]
- **Dragonglass:** The primary weapon that defeated the Others, according to legend. Also called "the dragonsteel" in some versions — which may refer to Valyrian steel, or may be a separate legendary material. [book_asoiaf]
- **The Last Hero:** A legendary figure who sought the children of the forest for help during the Long Night. His story varies by telling. [book_asoiaf]

## The Children of the Forest

The children of the forest are an ancient non-human species who inhabited Westeros before the First Men arrived. They carved the faces into the weirwood trees. They fought the First Men, made a pact with them at the Isle of Faces, and largely retreated to their strongholds. During the Long Night, according to legend, the children fought alongside men against the Others.

In the show, it is revealed that the children of the forest created the Others — they drove obsidian into the heart of a captured First Man at a weirwood tree, creating the first White Walker as a weapon against the First Men, who were cutting down the weirwoods. The weapon escaped their control. [show_got_s5_8]

The books imply this origin but do not confirm it. The show's explicit version may or may not match what Martin has planned. [book_asoiaf — implied, show_s5_8 for explicit confirmation]

## Azor Ahai

The hero of the Long Night in R'hllor's faith — a figure who forged a sword called Lightbringer by plunging it into the heart of his beloved wife Nissa Nissa. The sword burned with magical fire and drove back the darkness. Azor Ahai is expected to be reborn as "the prince that was promised" — a messianic figure who will use Lightbringer in the second Long Night.

Melisandre believed Stannis was Azor Ahai reborn. She later believed Jon Snow might be. Both interpretations relied on the same ambiguous prophecy read through the flames. [book_asoiaf]

## The Second Long Night (Show)

In the show, the second Long Night was averted at the Battle of Winterfell, when Arya Stark killed the Night King. The army of the dead collapsed. The threat ended. [show_got_s5_8]

Whether the books treat this as a single climactic battle or as a longer, more complex conflict is unknown. The Winds of Winter and A Dream of Spring would need to be published to know. Martin has said the ending of the books will differ from the show's in certain ways; whether the White Walker threat ends differently is among the largest open questions.
