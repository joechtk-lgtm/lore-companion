# The Battle of the Gullet

## Overview

The Battle of the Gullet was a naval engagement fought during the Dance of the Dragons — one of the largest sea battles in Westerosi history. The Gullet is the strait between Dragonstone and Driftmark, the passage into Blackwater Bay. A Triarchy fleet (ships from the Free Cities of Lys, Myr, and Tyrosh, allied with the Greens) sailed to blockade it, cutting off the Blacks' access to their most important naval base and threatening Dragonstone itself. The Blacks met them with the Velaryon fleet and with dragons. The Triarchy fleet was broken and largely destroyed. Jacaerys Velaryon, Rhaenyra's eldest son and her heir, died above the battle. [book_fire_blood]

## Why It Happened

The Triarchy had been enemies of the Targaryens during Daemon's Stepstones campaign years earlier. When the Dance began, they saw opportunity — a weakened Westeros fighting itself, the Greens in need of naval allies. They committed their fleet to the Greens' cause. Their goal: close the Gullet, isolate Dragonstone, and force the Blacks to fight on land where the Greens had more conventional advantages. [book_fire_blood]

## The Battle

The Velaryon fleet, the finest in Westeros, engaged the Triarchy in the strait. The Blacks had the advantage of dragons above the fleet — Jacaerys Velaryon on Vermax, and other dragonriders. Dragon fire from above broke the formation of the Triarchy fleet, which had never faced dragons in open battle.

The Triarchy fleet was destroyed. The blocking force that was supposed to isolate Dragonstone never completed its mission. Blackwater Bay remained accessible to the Blacks. By conventional measures, it was a decisive Black victory. [book_fire_blood]

## Jacaerys Velaryon

Jacaerys was Rhaenyra's firstborn son — the child whose legitimacy was questioned because his father was technically Laenor Velaryon but whose appearance suggested Harwin Strong. He was already a proven diplomat; he had traveled to Winterfell and the Vale to secure alliances for his mother at significant personal risk. He rode Vermax, a young adult dragon.

Above the Gullet, he died. The accounts of Fire & Blood diverge on exactly how: some say Vermax was dragged down by sailors who caught his wing in the rigging; some say he was hit by scorpion bolts. The battle was chaotic, the accounts unreliable.

The result was unambiguous: Jacaerys and Vermax were gone. [book_fire_blood]

## Significance

The Battle of the Gullet encapsulates the paradox of the Dance. The Blacks won the battle and lost the heir. Their dragon-based advantage was real but not invulnerable — a battle environment of ships, rigging, and desperate men with weapons could kill a dragonrider even when it could not stop the fleet they were protecting.

After Jacaerys's death, Rhaenyra had no obvious heir. Her sons Lucerys (already dead by this point, killed by Aemond), Joffrey (who died attempting to claim an unbroken dragon), and her Daemon-born sons were younger and less prepared. The Blacks were winning battles and losing the war by attrition — every dragonrider they lost could not be replaced, and the Greens needed only to survive long enough to find that the Blacks had run out of riders. [book_fire_blood]

## Aftermath

The Triarchy was broken as a naval force and withdrew from the conflict. The Gullet remained in Black hands. But the death of Jacaerys rippled through Rhaenyra's court — he had been the stable heir, the son who had done diplomatic work, the child who showed the most promise for the kind of ruler she needed to leave behind.

His death accelerated the desperate search for new dragonriders that would lead to the Dragonseeds — common people of Valyrian blood from Dragonstone and Driftmark who could potentially bond with the remaining unmounted dragons. Some succeeded. Some were burned alive for the attempt. This gamble was what the Blacks were reduced to by the war's middle stage: recruiting untrained dragonriders to replace the dead.
