# Tywin Lannister

## Overview

Tywin Lannister is the richest man in Westeros, the greatest military commander of his generation, and the most powerful political force in the realm for most of the story. He is also a man whose obsession with legacy produced three children — one who murders and cruelties his way through adolescence, one who conducts an incestuous affair producing heirs with no legitimate claim, and one who is widely acknowledged a dwarf and whose existence Tywin views as a personal affront. The Lannister legacy he worked his entire life to build rests on foundations he refuses to examine. He does not love; he invests. The people around him are instruments for building something that outlasts him.

He dies on a privy, shot with a crossbow by the son he spent decades treating as a mistake.

## Key Facts

- **House:** Lannister, Lord of Casterly Rock, Warden of the West, Shield of Lannisport, Hand of the King (multiple times). [book_asoiaf]
- **His achievement:** He restored the Lannister name. His father, Tytos Lannister, was weak — mocked by his bannermen, unable to collect debts. Tywin, at fifteen, personally led the forces that brought rebellious bannermen to heel. He made the Lannisters feared. [book_asoiaf]
- **Casterly Rock:** The seat of House Lannister and one of the most impregnable fortresses in Westeros, built into a massive rock formation. The gold mines beneath it made the Lannisters the wealthiest family in the realm. [book_asoiaf]
- **His wife:** Joanna Lannister, who died giving birth to Tyrion. Tywin loved her genuinely and never recovered from her loss. He blamed Tyrion for it in a way that is entirely irrational and entirely consistent with his character. [book_asoiaf]
- **His personal motto:** He did not believe in "A Lannister always pays his debts" as a motto — he considered it vulgar. The official words are "Hear Me Roar." He paid his debts because it was useful to be feared, not as a principle. [book_asoiaf]

## Relationships

- **Cersei:** He respected her intelligence and controlled her regardless. His refusal to allow her formal power was consistent with his worldview about women in governance. She resented it enormously. [book_asoiaf]
- **Jaime:** His heir, the son who should have carried the legacy. Jaime joined the Kingsguard — taking himself out of the succession — as a deliberate move by Aerys to steal Tywin's heir, and Jaime accepted it partly to escape the weight of his father's expectations. Tywin never forgave it. [book_asoiaf]
- **Tyrion:** The child he cannot look at without seeing his wife's death and his own shame. He used Tyrion when useful and sidelined him otherwise. He sentenced him to death. He would have let him die. [book_asoiaf]
- **Olenna Tyrell:** A peer. The one person whose political acumen he genuinely respected. Their alliance after the Blackwater was shrewd from both sides. [book_asoiaf] [show_got_s1_4]

## The Red Wedding

Tywin Lannister planned the Red Wedding. He provided the conditions — Roose Bolton's willingness to betray Robb for Warden of the North, Walder Frey's grievance about the broken betrothal and desire for legitimacy — and he orchestrated the timing and support. He did not attend. He sat in his solar when the reports came in. He treated it as a military operation with satisfactory results. [book_asoiaf] [show_got_s1_4]

He was at King's Landing when Tyrion arrived in his chambers with a crossbow. He called Shae a whore to Tyrion's face. He was shot twice. He died on the privy. The master of Westeros's political game died in the most undignified possible setting, killed by the child he spent decades discarding. [book_asoiaf] [show_got_s1_4]

## Legacy

Tywin's death was immediately destabilizing. He had been the center that held — his competence, his fear factor, his ability to manage Cersei and position the Lannisters. Without him, Cersei made increasingly erratic decisions. The Casterly Rock gold mines were beginning to run dry (the books note this). The debts to the Iron Bank were accumulating. The Lannister power structure was overextended and relied on a single man to hold it together. That man was dead on a privy, killed by his own crossbow, and the machinery he built began to disassemble.

"A lion does not concern himself with the opinions of the sheep." He was wrong about this, in the end. The opinions of the sheep — Robb Stark's, the north's, Tyrion's — concerned him a great deal, as it turned out, and cost him everything. [book_asoiaf]
