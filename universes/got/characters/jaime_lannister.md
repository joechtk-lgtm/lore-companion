# Jaime Lannister

## Overview

Ser Jaime Lannister is called the Kingslayer, and he has carried that title like a brand for seventeen years. The word means coward, oath-breaker, man without honor — and he earned it by killing the king he swore to protect. The truth is more complicated. It is always more complicated with Jaime. He killed King Aerys II Targaryen to prevent Aerys from destroying King's Landing with wildfire. He saved the city. He told no one. He sat down on the throne he could have claimed and waited for Ned Stark to arrive and find him there, and he let Ned's judgment stand. He has been the Kingslayer ever since, and the one time he tried to explain — to Catelyn Stark in the baths at Harrenhal — it was the first time he had told anyone in seventeen years, and he promptly passed out from fever.

He is not simply a villain redeemed. He is a complicated man whose best act became his worst reputation.

## Key Facts

- **House:** Lannister of Casterly Rock. [book_asoiaf]
- **Twin:** Cersei Lannister. The incest began when they were children. [book_asoiaf]
- **Status:** Kingsguard since age fifteen — he was appointed by Aerys to spite Tywin, who had arranged his appointment as a hostage maneuver. The youngest Kingsguard ever named. [book_asoiaf]
- **Reputation:** The best swordsman in Westeros for most of his adult life. Arthur Dayne was his only acknowledged superior. [book_asoiaf]
- **The Kingsguard vow:** No lands, no titles, no wife, no children. No inheritance. His appointment removed him from Tywin's succession. Tywin never forgave it. [book_asoiaf]
- **The Book of Brothers:** The Kingsguard keeps a record of each member's deeds. As of the series, Jaime's entry is almost blank — the recorded deeds of a man who sold his honor. He knows. [book_asoiaf] [show_s1_4]

## Relationships

- **Cersei:** His twin, his lover, the center of his world for most of his life. Her increasing ruthlessness and his growing moral clarity push them apart. [book_asoiaf]
- **Tywin:** His father, who used him as a political piece from the beginning — appointed to the Kingsguard as a maneuver, freed from it as another. Tywin's contempt for Tyrion extended to using Jaime as its opposite. Jaime eventually freed Tyrion from the cells, which Tywin would have viewed as the ultimate betrayal. [book_asoiaf]
- **Brienne of Tarth:** The knight who escorted him from Riverrun to King's Landing after his capture. They began as contempt and ended as genuine respect — the bath confession was the turning point. She received Oathkeeper, the sword reforged from Ice. He sent her to protect Sansa. [book_asoiaf] [show_s1_4]
- **Tyrion:** The brother he genuinely loves. The revelation about Tysha — the truth he should have told decades earlier — broke the bond in a specific way, while the act of freeing him from the dungeons preserved something. [book_asoiaf]

## The Truth of the Kingslaying

King Aerys II Targaryen had spent years descending into paranoia and cruelty. By the time of Robert's Rebellion, he was burning people alive in the throne room for entertainment, having sexual violence done to Rhaella after the burnings, keeping wildfire caches throughout King's Landing. When Tywin Lannister opened the gates of King's Landing to Robert's forces — betraying the king he served — Aerys commanded his pyromancer Rossart to detonate the caches. The city would burn rather than be taken.

Jaime killed Rossart first. Then he killed Aerys. He sat on the Iron Throne when the Stark bannermen found him and said nothing, offered nothing, because he knew no one would believe him and he had learned enough about the world to not try explaining himself to it.

He has been judged for it ever since. He does not argue. He knows the story will never change. [book_asoiaf]

## Capture at the Whispering Wood

Robb Stark's forces set an ambush at the Whispering Wood, drawing Jaime's cavalry into a trap. Jaime fought his way toward Robb personally, killing men to reach him, and was taken. He spent the better part of a year as a prisoner of Robb Stark, held at Riverrun. He attempted escape once, killing two guards. He was recaptured. [book_asoiaf] [show_s1_4]

Catelyn Stark eventually freed him in exchange for a promise to return her daughters — a unilateral decision that damaged her relationship with Robb. She sent Brienne of Tarth to escort him south.

## The Road with Brienne and the Loss of His Hand

The journey from Riverrun to King's Landing became the most consequential period of Jaime's life. At Harrenhal's baths, delirious with fever, he told Brienne the truth about Aerys. She was one of the few people who responded with something other than contempt. The exchange changed both of them.

Before reaching King's Landing, they were captured by Locke, a Bolton bannerman. Jaime's sword hand was cut off and hung around his neck.

He arrived in King's Landing a broken version of himself. Without his sword hand, the identity he had built — the finest swordsman in Westeros — was gone. He began training with his left hand under the instruction of Ser Ilyn Payne, in secret. He received Oathkeeper from Tywin (the Valyrian steel reforged from Ice), which he gave to Brienne with the charge to find and protect Sansa Stark. [book_asoiaf] [show_s1_4]

## Estrangement and the Riverlands

After Tywin's death and Cersei's increasing erratic behavior, Jaime's relationship with his sister deteriorated. She demanded things from him — loyalty, support, presence — that he found himself less able to give. He was sent to the Riverlands to resolve the continuing holdouts of Lannister power: the siege of Riverrun, negotiations with Edmure Tully. In the books he is still in the Riverlands at the end of ADwD; Brienne appears and leads him somewhere. Where is not yet published.

In the show, he returns to Cersei rather than abandoning her as his book arc seems to be building toward. [show_s5_8]

## Show Ending

In the show, Jaime fights at the Battle of Winterfell against the White Walkers, then rides south to be with Cersei despite Brienne begging him to stay. He reaches King's Landing as Daenerys destroys it. He and Cersei die together in the collapse of the Red Keep. [show_s5_8]

The book trajectory is expected to differ significantly. Jaime's arc in the novels has been building toward a break from Cersei, not a reconciliation. His final chapter in the published books leaves his fate ambiguous and suggests he will not simply return to her. The Winds of Winter is unpublished; his ending is unknown.
