# Cersei Lannister

## Overview

Cersei Lannister is Queen of the Seven Kingdoms, then Queen Regent, then Queen in her own right. She is intelligent in the way that isolated, paranoid people are intelligent — she reads situations correctly about half the time and catastrophically overcorrects the other half. Her children are the one genuine force in her life. Everything she does is done for them, which makes her capable of tremendous cruelty toward anyone perceived as a threat to them, and tremendous vulnerability to anything that actually threatens them. She is not simply villainous. She is a person who was placed in a position of power through marriage, denied formal authority in a patriarchal society, watched every man around her be handed what she wanted, and decided to take it anyway by any means available.

## Key Facts

- **House:** Lannister by birth and Baratheon by marriage. [book_asoiaf]
- **Twin:** Jaime Lannister, her twin brother and her lover from childhood. The incest is an open secret by midway through the story. [book_asoiaf]
- **Children:** Joffrey, Myrcella, Tommen — fathered by Jaime, passed as Robert's. [book_asoiaf]
- **The prophecy:** Maggy the Frog, a woods witch Cersei visited as a girl, told her she would marry the king, outlive her children, and be destroyed by a younger and more beautiful queen. She has spent her entire life trying to prevent the last part and failing at every step. [book_asoiaf]
- **Her political style:** Networks of informants, strategic marriages for her children, elimination of rivals through legal or extralegal means. She is better than she is usually given credit for and worse than she imagines herself to be. [book_asoiaf]
- **Her relationship with Robert:** She loved him once, in the beginning. He called out Lyanna's name on their wedding night. Whatever feeling existed died quickly. Their marriage became a mutual exercise in endurance. [book_asoiaf]

## Relationships

- **Jaime:** The center of her world for most of her life. The relationship becomes increasingly complicated by his absences, his changes after losing his sword hand, and his growing moral complexity that she experiences as betrayal. [book_asoiaf]
- **Tywin:** She admired him more than she loved him and feared him at the same time. She understood what he was. After his death, she found she had been relying on his competence without realizing it. [book_asoiaf]
- **Tyrion:** She blames him for their mother's death. The hatred is genuine and irrational in proportion to its cause, which she is aware of and cannot moderate. [book_asoiaf]
- **Margaery Tyrell:** The younger and more beautiful queen the prophecy warned her about. Cersei's treatment of Margaery is the clearest illustration of how her paranoia makes her create the threats she fears. [book_asoiaf]

## As Queen Regent

After Robert's death and through Joffrey's reign, Cersei held actual power while Tyrion and later Tywin held the title of Hand. The conflict was constant. She undermined Tyrion at every turn; he outmaneuvered her more often than she acknowledged. After the Blackwater, Tywin took the Handship directly and marginalized her — she had authority as queen regent but found her father's presence limited it severely. [book_asoiaf] [show_s1_4]

## The War's Management

Cersei's management of the war was competent in small things and disastrous in large ones. She secured the Tyrell alliance through Joffrey's betrothal to Margaery. She maintained the court through the siege of King's Landing while Joffrey sulked in his chambers. She kept the city from collapsing in the immediate aftermath. She also, in the books, arranged the assassination attempt on Tyrion at the Blackwater — a decision born of paranoia that damaged her position when it failed. [book_asoiaf]

[spoiler_tier: red_wedding]
## After the Red Wedding

Tywin's brilliant alliance with the Freys and Boltons ended Robb Stark's war. Cersei benefited enormously but received none of the credit — Tywin distributed it to himself and his allies. Joffrey died at his own wedding. Tyrion was convicted. Tywin was killed by Tyrion. In a very short span, Cersei lost her father, her eldest child, and her most competent political ally. She began ruling alone. [book_asoiaf] [show_s1_4]

She did not rule well. She recalled Kevan Lannister and then alienated him. She relied on the Faith Militant without understanding what she was empowering. She played Margaery against Tommen's Tyrell loyalty and lost.

[spoiler_tier: dance_of_dragons]
## The Faith Militant and the Walk of Shame

Cersei, attempting to use the newly armed Faith Militant against the Tyrells, was outmaneuvered: the High Sparrow arrested Margaery for adultery — and then arrested Cersei for the same and for the incest with Jaime that had been an open secret. She was imprisoned in the Great Sept of Baelor and forced to confess. Her penance was the Walk of Shame: naked, shorn, walking through King's Landing while the crowd jeered. [book_asoiaf] [show_s5_8]

It did not break her. It made her more dangerous. She emerged from it with the desire for revenge fully in control of her judgment. She began planning the use of the wildfire caches beneath the city. [book_asoiaf for this trajectory; show_s5_8 for execution]

[spoiler_tier: show_complete]
## The Sept and the End

Cersei destroyed the Sept of Baelor with wildfire, killing the High Sparrow, Margaery, Loras, Kevan Lannister, and hundreds of others. Tommen saw it from the window and stepped off the ledge. With her last child dead, she took the Iron Throne for herself, crowned queen without a king. [show_s5_8]

She died in the collapse of the Red Keep during Daenerys's assault on King's Landing, buried under rubble beside Jaime, who had returned to be with her at the end. [show_s5_8]

The book arc for Cersei remains unresolved. The Winds of Winter chapters released show her facing the Faith Militant's trial by combat; her trajectory beyond that is unknown.
