# Palantíri (The Seeing Stones)

## Overview

The palantíri — the Seeing Stones, the Seven Stones — are ancient artifacts made in Valinor by Fëanor in the Elder Days, brought to Middle-earth by the Númenóreans, distributed among the kingdoms of Gondor and Arnor. They are crystal spheres of varying size, perfectly smooth, dark in color until activated, and they allow communication — anyone who can master a stone may look into it and see distant places and events, or communicate with another person looking into a linked stone. They are not simply communication devices; using them requires strength of will, and Sauron turned that requirement into a trap.

## Key Facts

- **Operation:** A palantír shows what is in its range or what another stone chooses to show. It cannot be compelled to show falsehood directly — but it can be directed to show a true thing in a misleading context, a true image that creates a false impression. This is how Sauron used it. [book_lotr]
- **The Seven Stones:** Originally distributed between Gondor (four stones: Osgiliath, Minas Anor, Minas Ithil, Orthanc) and Arnor (three stones: Annúminas, Amon Sûl, Elostirion). The Osgiliath stone was lost in the Kin-strife. The Minas Ithil stone fell to Sauron when the Nazgûl took the tower. [book_lotr] [book_silmarillion]
- **The Stone of Orthanc:** Saruman had the Orthanc stone, which he used to communicate with Sauron — and was thereby slowly dominated. He believed he was using Sauron; Sauron understood from the beginning that Saruman had become his instrument. [book_lotr]
- **Denethor and the Anor Stone:** Denethor had the Minas Anor (Anor) stone and used it in secret for decades, believing he could master it. He had the strength to resist direct domination — but Sauron used the stone to show him true but despairing images of Mordor's power and the West's weakness. This is what broke Denethor's hope. [book_lotr]
- **Pippin and the Orthanc Stone:** Pippin looks into the Orthanc stone impulsively after Wormtongue throws it from the tower. Sauron sees him and assumes he is the Ringbearer, which produces false information that actually helps the Fellowship. Gandalf takes the stone from him. [book_lotr] [film_two_towers]

## Aragorn and the Stone

Aragorn uses the Anor stone intentionally before the Battle of the Pelennor Fields, revealing himself to Sauron as the heir of Isildur and claiming the right to use it. This is a massive provocation designed to distract Sauron and convince him that Aragorn is the greatest threat — drawing his attention away from Frodo. It works, at the cost of what Aragorn sees in the stone (which he does not share). [book_lotr]
