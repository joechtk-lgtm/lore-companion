# The Light of Eärendil

## Overview

Eärendil the Mariner — half-elven, the son of Tuor (a Man) and Idril (an Elf), husband of Elwing — is one of the most important figures in Tolkien's mythology, though he appears directly in neither The Lord of the Rings nor The Hobbit. He is the father of Elrond and Elros, the ancestor of Aragorn through Elros, and his voyage to Valinor is the act that ended the First Age and saved Middle-earth from Morgoth. He sails now as a star — the brightest star in the night sky — bearing the Silmaril on his brow. From his light the phial of Galadriel is filled, and that phial saves Frodo's life.

## Eärendil the Mariner

- **The Voyage:** Eärendil, bearing the Silmaril he and Elwing had recovered, sailed to Valinor — a journey no mortal or half-mortal was permitted to make. He came as a messenger and a suppliant, begging the Valar to aid Middle-earth against Morgoth. His plea was heard. The War of Wrath followed; Morgoth was overthrown. [book_silmarillion]
- **The Fate of Eärendil:** Because he had come to Valinor as a mortal (technically), he and Elwing were given the choice of the half-elven. Eärendil chose Elvish immortality — but he could not return to Middle-earth. He was set in the sky to sail through the heavens, the Silmaril on his brow, as a sign of hope to those who dwell in Middle-earth. [book_silmarillion]
- **The Silmaril:** The Silmarilli were the three jewels made by Fëanor in Valinor, containing the light of the Two Trees before they were destroyed by Morgoth. They are the MacGuffin of the entire First Age. Eärendil's Silmaril is the one that survives — the others were lost in sea and earth. It gives his star its particular brightness. [book_silmarillion]

## The Phial of Galadriel

Galadriel gives Frodo, as his departing gift from Lothlórien, a small crystal vial containing water in which the light of Eärendil's star has been caught. "May it be a light to you in dark places, when all other lights go out." [book_lotr]

The phial is used in Shelob's lair — Frodo holds it up in total darkness and it blazes with blinding light, driving Shelob back. The light comes through in an unbroken chain: the light of the Silmaril, which was made from the light of the Two Trees of Valinor, which were made from the light of Telperion and Laurelin, the first great lights of the world. [book_lotr]

## "Aiya Eärendil elenion ancalima!"

When Frodo cries out in Shelob's lair, his words are Elvish: "Hail Eärendil, brightest of stars!" It is an ancient invocation, from the deep history of the First Age. Frodo does not fully understand what he is saying; the words come from something older than his conscious knowledge. This is one of the moments where Tolkien explicitly connects the small-scale story of the hobbits to the vast mythology behind it. [book_lotr] [book_silmarillion]
