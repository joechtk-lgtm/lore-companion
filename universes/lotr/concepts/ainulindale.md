# The Ainulindalë (The Music of the Ainur)

## Overview

The Ainulindalë — the Music of the Ainur, the Great Music — is the creation myth of Tolkien's world: the account of how Eä (the universe) came into being through music, how the Ainur (angelic beings created by Ilúvatar, the One, the supreme deity) participated in that creation, and how Morgoth's corruption entered the world at the moment of its making.

It is the foundational document of Tolkien's mythology, the opening of the Silmarillion, and essential for understanding why evil exists in Middle-earth in the particular form it takes.

## Key Facts

- **Ilúvatar:** The One — the supreme deity, the creator of the Ainur and of the Children of Ilúvatar (Elves and Men). He does not appear in Middle-earth; he acts through the Ainur and through the laws he set at the beginning. In some respects he corresponds to a monotheistic God. [book_silmarillion]
- **The Ainur:** Created beings — pure spirit — who sang the world into existence. The greater Ainur who entered the world (Eä) to shape it are the Valar; the lesser are the Maiar. Both Gandalf and Sauron are Maiar; so is Balrog Durin's Bane. Morgoth (Melkor) was the greatest of the Ainur. [book_silmarillion]
- **The Music:** Ilúvatar proposed a theme to the Ainur, and they sang it forth. Melkor (Morgoth), the greatest of them, wove his own themes into the Music — themes of pride and power — and others followed him. Ilúvatar responded by introducing new themes; the conflict of themes became the history of the world. [book_silmarillion]
- **The Vision:** After the Music, Ilúvatar showed the Ainur a vision of the world as the Music had shaped it. Then he gave it being: "Eä! Let these things be!" — and the world existed. [book_silmarillion]
- **Why Evil Exists:** Tolkien's answer to this question is built into his cosmology. Morgoth's discord in the Music was not an accident or a failure of Ilúvatar's plan; it was woven into the Music itself, and Ilúvatar had already countered it and incorporated it into the greater pattern. Evil exists, causes real harm, but cannot ultimately prevail against the pattern of creation. This does not make the harm less real. [book_silmarillion]

## Significance for the Story

Understanding the Ainulindalë explains things that otherwise seem arbitrary in the main story:
- Why Sauron and Gandalf are described as "the same kind of being" — both are Maiar.
- Why Balrogs are terrifying beyond ordinary monsters — they are also Maiar, corrupted by Morgoth.
- Why Galadriel and Elrond are old beyond reckoning — they arrived in Middle-earth before the rising of the Sun and Moon.
- Why the Valar seem distant and reluctant to intervene — they act through persuasion and preparation, not direct force, since the time when the world was to be governed by the Children. [book_silmarillion] [book_lotr]
