# The Istari (The Wizards)

## Overview

The Istari are five Maiar sent to Middle-earth by the Valar in the Third Age, approximately 1,000 years after the Third Age began, to assist the free peoples in their resistance to Sauron. They came in the bodies of old men — not as disguise but as limitation. Part of the terms of their mission was that they could not use their full power directly or dominate the free peoples. They were sent to inspire, counsel, and encourage. Three of the five failed their mission in different ways. Two fulfilled it.

## The Five

- **Gandalf (Olórin, Mithrandir)** — The Grey, then the White. Wisest of the Maiar. His mission was to kindle hope and inspire resistance. He succeeded. [book_silmarillion] [book_lotr]
- **Saruman (Curunír)** — The White. Head of the order. His mission was to coordinate the resistance and his specific expertise was in Rings and Sauron's methods. He was corrupted by ambition and his use of the Palantír. [book_lotr]
- **Radagast (Aiwendil)** — The Brown. Sent by Yavanna. His mission was the care of living things; he became so absorbed in animals and birds that he largely neglected the larger struggle. He was not evil — just distracted from his purpose. [book_lotr]
- **The Blue Wizards (Alatar and Pallando, or Morinehtar and Rómestámo)** — Sent into the east and south to stir up resistance to Sauron among the peoples there. Their fate is uncertain. Tolkien in his later writings suggested they may have been partly successful in founding resistance cults in the east, though they also may have gone astray and founded magical religions. [book_silmarillion] [extended]

## The Mission's Constraint

The Istari were prohibited from revealing their full power, by direct domination, or by replacing one Dark Lord with another. This is why Gandalf refuses the Ring when Frodo offers it — not from weakness but from this principled understanding. He knows that if he took it and wielded it, even for good ends, he would become exactly what he was sent to oppose. [book_lotr]

Saruman's failure is partly the failure to respect this constraint — he decided that a wizard great enough could wield the Ring, could oppose Sauron with Sauron's own instrument. This reasoning, which seems militarily sensible, is the exact corruption the constraint was designed to prevent. [book_lotr]
