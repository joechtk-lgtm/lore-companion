# The Elves (The Elder Kindred)

## Overview

The Elves were the First Children of Ilúvatar — the first of the sentient peoples to wake in Middle-earth, in the First Age, beside the waters of Cuiviénen. They are immortal in the sense of not dying from age or illness; they can be killed in battle or waste away from grief, but if they die their spirits go to the Halls of Mandos and eventually return. They were given the gift of craft, of memory, of song — they perceive the world more fully than mortal peoples. They also carry a particular grief: they are bound to the world as it fades, watching all they love diminish and pass away while they persist.

## The Kindreds

- **Vanyar** — The fairest of the Elves. Most remained in Valinor. Rarely seen in Middle-earth. [book_silmarillion]
- **Noldor** — The deep Elves, the craftsmen. They created the Silmarils; they forged the great works of the First Age. Many came to Middle-earth in the First Age and their descendants remain — Galadriel, Elrond, Círdan. [book_silmarillion]
- **Teleri / Sindar / Wood-elves** — The sea-Elves who remained at the coast or stayed in Middle-earth. The Sindar (Grey Elves) include the Elves of Lothlórien's deepest history and the Woodland Realm. The Wood-elves of Mirkwood are of this kindred. [book_silmarillion]

## Immortality and the Fading

Elvish immortality is not quite what it appears. Elves do not age, but they can tire of the world. The world changes around them and they remain. Every Elf who has lived since the First Age carries the weight of all they have seen and lost. Galadriel has watched kingdoms rise and fall. The oldest Elves in Middle-earth at the time of the War of the Ring are worn thin by history.

More significantly: the Third Age is the age of Elvish fading. The Three Rings were made to preserve what the Elves loved — Lothlórien, Rivendell, the havens. They slow the passage of time, maintain the beauty of the Elder Days in diminished corners of the world. When the One Ring is destroyed, the Three Rings lose their power. Lothlórien and Rivendell will fade. The Elves who remain will find Middle-earth increasingly unlike what they loved. They sail West. [book_lotr]

## The Choice to Remain or Depart

The Elvish choice throughout the Third Age is essentially: go west now, while the Undying Lands offer what Middle-earth no longer can, or stay and fight. Those who stay — even for good reasons — watch their communities dwindle. Those who go leave the world to the younger peoples. Both choices carry grief. The Grey Havens at Mithlond are the departure point. Círdan the Shipwright has been building ships since the Second Age. [book_lotr] [book_silmarillion]
