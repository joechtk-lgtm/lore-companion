# The Nazgûl (The Nine Ringwraiths)

## Overview

The Nazgûl — Ring-wraiths, the Nine, the Black Riders, the Úlairi — are the nine mortal Men to whom Sauron gave Rings of Power in the Second Age. They were kings, sorcerers, and swordlords of great wealth and ambition: three were of Númenorean descent, one was the greatest sorcerer of the age, and others were lords of Men in the east and south. The Rings gave them power, wealth, the undying bodies they sought. They also slowly consumed them until they became wraiths — wholly enslaved to the One Ring and its maker, visible in the world of living things only as black-robed shadows and the memory of what they once were.

## Key Facts

- **The process:** The Rings did not corrupt immediately. They gave what they promised — power, long life, the ability to bend men to their will. But the wearer faded: they became ever more powerful in the wraith-world and ever less real in the physical world, until they passed entirely into shadow. [book_lotr] [book_silmarillion]
- **The Witch-king of Angmar:** The Lord of the Nazgûl, the most powerful of the Nine. He was a great king in the Second Age; by the Third Age he was the destroyer of Arnor, the captain of Sauron's armies. The Witch-king cannot be killed by a man — this is his specific doom or protection, prophesied at Weathertop and fulfilled at the Pelennor. He was laid low by Éowyn and Merry. [book_lotr]
- **Khamûl:** The second of the Nine, called the Black Easterling. He was the most dangerous of the Nazgûl after the Witch-king. He dwelt in Dol Guldur after Sauron was driven out, and was among the Nazgûl who searched the Shire for Frodo. [book_lotr] [extended]
- **Weapons:** Their chief weapon is fear — they spread the Black Breath, a spiritual dread that can paralyze and, over time, kill. Their morgul-blades leave fragments in wounds that slowly turn the victim toward wraithood. The blades themselves shatter upon completion of their purpose. [book_lotr]
- **What they were:** Tolkien does not name most of the Nine. They were great men once. That is exactly the point: the Rings took greatness and consumed it. [book_lotr]

## The Hunt for the Ring

The Nine are dispatched from Mordor when Sauron learns the Ring has been found in the Shire. They come as Black Riders, wearing cloaks and riding black horses (which they terrify into service). They reach the Shire, trace Frodo to Crickhollow and Bree, and pursue the company to Weathertop. At Weathertop, the Witch-king wounds Frodo with a Morgul-blade. [book_lotr] [film_fellowship]

[spoiler_tier: gondor]
## Their End

At the Battle of the Pelennor Fields, the Witch-king is killed by Éowyn and Merry. The remaining eight Nazgûl are scattered when the Ring is destroyed at Mount Doom. Without Sauron and without the One Ring, their own rings are broken. They have no existence independent of the Ring's power. They are consumed with it, dissolving into final nothing.
