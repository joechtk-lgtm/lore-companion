# Hobbits (The Shire-folk)

## Overview

Hobbits are a small people — three to four feet tall, broad in the shoulder, with large hairy feet and a preference for comfort, good food, pipe-weed, and a life undisturbed by the wider world. They are of the race of Men in the broad sense but have diverged significantly — they are smaller, longer-lived than most Men, quieter in movement, and curiously resistant to certain forms of magic and compulsion. Their resistance to the Ring's direct domination is one of their defining qualities and the reason Gandalf chose a hobbit as Ring-bearer.

## History

The hobbits have been in the Shire for only about 1,600 years by the time of the War of the Ring — they crossed the Brandywine from the east in the Third Age and settled the fertile land granted to them by the King of Arnor (then still a functioning kingdom). Before that, they lived in the valley of the Anduin, and before that their origins are obscure even to them. [book_lotr]

## The Three Kindreds

- **Harfoots** — The most common, the most typical hobbit. Fond of hills and highlands. They are what most people mean when they say hobbit. [book_lotr]
- **Stoors** — Larger, heavier-footed. Fond of water. Gollum was of the Stoor-kind. [book_lotr]
- **Fallohides** — Tallest and fairest. More adventurous and friendly with Elves and Men. The Tooks and Brandybucks carry Fallohide blood — which is why they produce hobbits like Bilbo, Frodo, Merry, and Pippin who are slightly less satisfied with ordinary hobbit life than their neighbors. [book_lotr]

## Why Gandalf Chose Them

Tolkien's hobbits were deliberately constructed as an unlikely people for great deeds — small, comfort-loving, unambitious, unheroic in any conventional sense. Gandalf's selection of a hobbit as Ring-bearer was based on a specific calculation: the Ring works by amplifying the desire for power in its bearer. Hobbits, with their limited ambition and their attachment to small pleasures, are less susceptible to this amplification. They do not want to rule the world. The Ring has less to offer them — and therefore less leverage. [book_lotr]

Sam's brief carrying of the Ring is the proof: he envisions a garden, not an empire. The Ring offered him the dream and he recognized it as a dream and set it down. [book_lotr]
