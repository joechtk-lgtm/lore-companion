# The Fall of Númenor (The Downfall, Atalantë)

## Overview

The Downfall of Númenor — Atalantë in Elvish, "the fallen one" — is the second great catastrophe of Tolkien's mythology (after the War of Wrath that ended the First Age) and the event that separates the Second Age from the Third. It destroyed the greatest civilization of Men, changed the shape of the world, removed Valinor from the mortal world's reach, and produced the exiles who founded Gondor and Arnor — the kingdoms whose fate dominates the Third Age story.

## The Causes

- **The Ban:** From Númenor's founding, the Valar had forbidden the Númenóreans from sailing west toward Valinor. Proximity to the Undying Lands did not mean access to their gift — the Valar could not give Men immortality, and the Ban was partly to prevent the Númenóreans from seeing what they could not have. As Númenor's power grew and its kings' lives shortened (they declined over generations from Elros' five hundred years to a more typical three hundred), the Ban became increasingly resented. [book_silmarillion]
- **Sauron's capture and corruption:** Ar-Pharazôn, the last and mightiest king, brought Sauron to Númenor as a prisoner after he failed to resist the king's fleet. Within three years, Sauron had become the king's chief counselor. He corrupted the Númenórean religion, turning them toward the worship of Morgoth (Sauron's former master), introducing human sacrifice, and systematically eliminating the "Faithful" — those Númenóreans who remained loyal to the Valar and the old ways. [book_silmarillion]
- **The Armada:** Sauron's final manipulation was to convince Ar-Pharazôn that seizing Valinor by force would grant immortality. The king built the greatest fleet in history and sailed west. [book_silmarillion]

## The Catastrophe

When Ar-Pharazôn's armada landed on the shores of Valinor, the Valar laid down their guardianship of the world and called on Ilúvatar directly. Ilúvatar changed the world's shape: Valinor and the Undying Lands were removed from the circles of the world — no longer reachable by any normal sailing, existing in a dimension apart. The seas were made bent: the world became round. Númenor was swallowed by the sea in a great cataclysm. The armada was buried under falling hills. [book_silmarillion]

Sauron's body was destroyed in the Downfall — he had left his ring in Middle-earth, which is why he survived at all. He returned to Middle-earth in purely terrible form, without the ability to take a fair shape again.

## The Survivors: The Faithful

Elendil (whose name means "Elf-friend" or "devoted to the stars"), who had kept his loyalty to the Valar and his ships in the eastern harbor, survived with his sons Isildur and Anárion and their households. They bore with them the seedling of the White Tree, the palantíri, and the sword Narsil. They landed in Middle-earth and founded the realms in exile: Gondor in the south, Arnor in the north. [book_silmarillion] [book_lotr]

## The Long Consequence

The entire political and cultural landscape of the Third Age is shaped by the Downfall: Gondor and Arnor are Númenórean realms. The Dúnedain (Men of the West) are the Faithful's descendants. Aragorn's kingship claim traces back to Elendil. Sauron's hostility to Gondor is personal — Gondor is the heir of his enemies, the ones who survived his masterwork. The fall of Númenor is the wound in history that the restoration of the Reunited Kingdom partially heals. [book_lotr] [book_silmarillion]
