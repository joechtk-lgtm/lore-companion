# Gandalf

## Overview

Gandalf is a wizard — one of the Istari, five Maiar sent to Middle-earth by the Valar in the Third Age to assist the free peoples against the shadow of Sauron. His true nature is not a man with magical powers but a Maia: an angelic being of immense age and capacity, incarnated in a mortal form as part of the terms of his mission. He is older than the world in any meaningful sense, having existed as a spirit in the music of the Ainur before the creation of Arda.

In Middle-earth, he appears as an old man in grey robes with a staff, wandering without fixed abode, known by many names to many peoples. He is Gandalf to the Hobbits and Men of the West; Mithrandir to the Elves (Grey Pilgrim); Olórin was his name in Valinor; Tharkûn to the Dwarves; Incánus in the south. He has been in Middle-earth for roughly two thousand years by the time of the War of the Ring.

## Key Facts

- **True nature:** Maia — one of the lesser Ainur, spiritual beings created before the world, sent by the Valar in physical form. In his true nature he is as the Valar themselves: immensely powerful, ancient beyond mortal comprehension. In his hobbit-form he is constrained by his mission. [book_silmarillion] [book_lotr]
- **Name in Valinor:** Olórin. He dwelt in the gardens of Lórien and Mandos, learning pity and wisdom. He was the wisest of the Maiar, though not the mightiest. [book_silmarillion]
- **His ring:** Narya, the Ring of Fire — one of the Three Elven Rings, given to him by Círdan the Shipwright when Gandalf arrived in Middle-earth. Narya kindles the fire of heart in those around him; it gives him the power to inspire. [book_silmarillion] [book_lotr]
- **The Grey and the White:** He arrives as Gandalf the Grey. After his battle with the Balrog in Moria — his death and return — he comes back as Gandalf the White, taking Saruman's place as head of the order of wizards. His power is greater; his embodied constraints are loosened by what he has passed through. [book_lotr]
- **Mission constraint:** The Istari were sent to Middle-earth with a specific prohibition: they may not use their power directly against Sauron or dominate the free peoples. They are sent to counsel, encourage, and inspire — not to act as gods. Saruman forgot this. Gandalf did not. [book_silmarillion]

## Relationships

- **Frodo:** He identified the Ring's nature, set the quest in motion, and trusted Frodo with it. His trust is not blind — it is based on decades of observing hobbits and on wisdom about what the Ring requires in a bearer: not power but the resistance that comes from having no desire for power. [book_lotr]
- **Bilbo:** He managed Bilbo's adventure in the Lonely Mountain, observed the Ring's first effects on Bilbo, and spent sixty years monitoring the situation before acting. Gandalf is patient in ways that mortals are not. [book_lotr] [book_hobbit]
- **Aragorn:** He identified Aragorn as the heir of Isildur, guided and guarded him for decades, and facilitated the reunification of the kingdoms of Men. The return of the King is partly Gandalf's work. [book_lotr]
- **The Balrog:** His death in Moria — his fall into the deep water, his battle with the Balrog, his return — is the hinge of his arc. He dies doing what the Istari were sent to do. His return as Gandalf the White marks his completion of his mission. [book_lotr] [film_fellowship]

## What He Knew Before the Quest

Gandalf has suspected the significance of Bilbo's ring for decades. He knew the Great Ring had been lost in the Gladden Fields when Isildur was ambushed. He observed the Ring's effect on Bilbo with growing concern. When Bilbo's ring began to feel like something that could not be let go, Gandalf began to investigate.

He examined it with fire. Tengwar letters appeared — the Ring-verse. This confirmed it. He spent the next seventeen years finding Gollum and extracting the history of the Ring's possession from him. Then he returned to the Shire. [book_lotr]

[spoiler_tier: rivendell]
## Moria

Gandalf knew the passage through Moria might mean his end. He went in anyway — there was no other way through. In the depths of Khazad-dûm, on the Bridge of Khazad-dûm, he faced the Balrog of Morgoth and fell with it into the abyss. He was dead, in the mortal sense, for a period of time that the text describes but does not fully quantify. He fought the Balrog through the deep water, up through the mountain's roots to the peak of Zirakzigil. After twenty days of battle, he cast the Balrog down and it broke the mountainside. Then Gandalf himself lay down and died. [book_lotr] [film_fellowship]

[spoiler_tier: rohan]
## Return as the White

He returned as Gandalf the White — sent back by the Valar, his mission not yet complete, his power enhanced. He breaks Saruman's hold on Théoden, leads the Rohirrim at Helm's Deep, organizes the defense of Gondor. As the White, he is not constrained in the same way the Grey was. He is essentially the functioning head of all resistance to Sauron in the field. [book_lotr] [film_two_towers]

[spoiler_tier: lotr_complete]
## Departure

When the Ring is destroyed and the Third Age ends, Gandalf's mission is complete. He sails from the Grey Havens with the other Ring-bearers. He does not remain in Middle-earth because Middle-earth is not where he belongs — he is a Maia, and his true home is in the Undying Lands. His departure is not a farewell; it is a return. [book_lotr]
