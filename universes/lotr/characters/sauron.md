# Sauron

## Overview

Sauron is the Dark Lord of the Third Age — but he is not the first Dark Lord, nor is he the greatest power of evil in the history of Arda. He was a Maia, of the order of Aulë the Smith, who was corrupted by Morgoth in the earliest ages of the world and became his most capable lieutenant. He survived Morgoth's defeat at the end of the First Age, repented briefly and then fled when called to account, and spent the Second Age building his own power in Middle-earth — culminating in the forging of the One Ring and the great war that left him defeated but not destroyed.

By the Third Age, he has lost his physical form but retains enough power to gather his servants, rebuild Mordor, and reach toward the dominion of Middle-earth again. He is not omnipotent. He is limited, in a critical way, by the Ring: he poured his will and power into it, and without it he is diminished. With it, he would be irresistible.

## Key Facts

- **True nature:** Maia of Aulë, corrupted by Morgoth. Older than the world in any meaningful sense. His original spirit was not evil; it was bent by Morgoth's persuasion toward order-through-domination rather than the free shaping of things. [book_silmarillion]
- **Annatar:** In the Second Age, he came to the Elven-smiths of Eregion in the form of a fair messenger calling himself Annatar, the Lord of Gifts. He taught them the craft of the Rings of Power. It was under this disguise that the Rings were forged. When the Elven smiths' rings were made, he secretly forged the One Ring to control them all. [book_silmarillion]
- **The One Ring:** He poured his will, his power, and a great portion of his own essence into the Ring. This is his strength and his weakness. The Ring amplifies his power enormously when he holds it. Its destruction means his destruction. [book_lotr] [book_silmarillion]
- **Physical form:** He lost his fair form when Númenor drowned — he was in Númenor and his physical body was destroyed. He could no longer take a fair shape afterward; he could only appear as a dark and terrible figure. By the Third Age, after his defeat at the end of the Second Age, he lacks a physical form entirely for a long time, existing as a shadow and a will. [book_silmarillion]
- **The Eye:** The image of Sauron in the Third Age — the lidless Eye, wreathed in flame — is how he reaches across the world without a body. It is also what the Palantíri show when they make contact with the Orthanc stone or the stone of Minas Ithil (now the Palantír of Barad-dûr). [book_lotr]
- **Servants:** The Nazgûl are his primary instruments. Beyond them: the Balrogs (the last of which is the Balrog of Moria), the hosts of Mordor, Saruman (compromised), and various other servants across Middle-earth. [book_lotr]

## The Shadow of Mordor

Sauron was defeated at the end of the Second Age but not destroyed — the Ring survived, and as long as the Ring survived, so did Sauron in some form. He retreated to Mordor after his defeat at the end of the Second Age, spending the first millennium of the Third Age as a formless shadow too weak to act openly. He re-emerged as the Necromancer in Dol Guldur in southern Mirkwood before his true identity was confirmed. When Gandalf drove him out of Dol Guldur, he withdrew to Mordor and began rebuilding openly. [book_lotr] [book_silmarillion]

## The End

When the Ring is destroyed in the fires of Mount Doom, Sauron's spirit is expelled from the world — not merely defeated but dissolved. He spent so much of himself in the Ring that without it there is nothing left capable of coherent form. The shadow over Mordor goes out. The tower of Barad-dûr falls. His servants are scattered or destroyed.

He will not come again. This is the end of the Dark Lord of the Third Age. [book_lotr] [film_return_king]

## History and Context

In the First Age, Sauron served Morgoth and was his greatest lieutenant — commanding Angband in Morgoth's absence, being the most versatile and capable of all Morgoth's servants. After Morgoth's defeat in the War of Wrath, Sauron initially appeared to repent, but when summoned to Valinor to stand judgment, he fled back to Middle-earth rather than face the Valar's sentence.

In the Second Age, his long game with the Elves of Eregion — teaching the craft of the Rings and then betraying it — represents his most sophisticated work. The near-domination of the free peoples of Middle-earth in the War of the Last Alliance was thwarted only by the specific act of Isildur cutting the Ring from his hand. He had not anticipated losing a finger. [book_silmarillion]
