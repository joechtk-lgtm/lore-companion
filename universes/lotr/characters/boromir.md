# Boromir

## Overview

Boromir is the eldest son of Denethor, Steward of Gondor, and the Captain-General of Gondor's armies. He is arguably the finest soldier in the Fellowship and among the most capable fighters in Middle-earth. He is also the Fellowship's most dangerous member in a specific sense: he wants to use the Ring. His desire is not for personal power — it is for the deliverance of his people from the slow grinding destruction of Sauron's pressure on Gondor's borders. He is not corrupt; he is human. The distinction matters.

His arc in the first book is the arc of a man with genuine virtue who is consumed by a single flaw and dies paying for it in the most redemptive way available to him.

## Key Facts

- **Position:** Captain-General of Gondor. Eldest son of Denethor II, Steward of Gondor. [book_lotr]
- **His mission:** He came to Rivendell following a prophetic dream shared with his brother Faramir — "Seek for the Sword that was Broken... Halfling forth shall stand." He came to understand the dream. He did not come to destroy the Ring. [book_lotr]
- **His argument:** He argues, at the Council of Elrond and throughout the journey, that the Ring is a weapon that should be used against Sauron. His argument is not foolish — it is the natural conclusion of a military mind. The counterargument (that no one can wield it without being corrupted by it) requires trusting a principle over military pragmatism. He cannot make that trust. [book_lotr]
- **His contrast with Faramir:** Tolkien specifically intended the Boromir/Faramir contrast — Boromir the bold soldier who cannot resist the Ring's temptation, Faramir the thoughtful captain who refuses it without struggle. Both are sons of Denethor; they show what Denethor's character and Gondor's values can produce at their best and their most vulnerable. [book_lotr]

## The Breaking

At Amon Hen, Boromir gives in. He follows Frodo and attempts to take the Ring by force — for Gondor, he says, and believes it. He is not entirely lying. But the Ring has amplified what was always there. Frodo puts the Ring on and flees. Boromir is left with what he has done.

He spends his last hour defending Merry and Pippin from Uruk-hai. He takes multiple arrows. He keeps fighting. He is found by Aragorn dying; he asks if the great quest has failed. Aragorn tells him not yet. "Then it is not so bad," says Boromir. He dies fighting for the thing he tried to betray. The redemption is real even if the textbook is closed.
