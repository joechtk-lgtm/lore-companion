# Gollum (Sméagol)

## Overview

Gollum was once Sméagol, a hobbit-like creature of the river-folk (the Stoors, one of the three hobbit-kindreds) who lived in the Vale of Anduin. He found the One Ring in the Gladden Fields approximately 2,500 years before the War of the Ring, murdered his friend Déagol to take it, was corrupted and exiled, and spent those centuries in the caves beneath the Misty Mountains, sustained by the Ring's power and eating raw fish and Goblinses. He is perhaps the most pitiful figure in Tolkien's work — a creature who was once capable of kindness and curiosity, wholly consumed by what he found, and yet whose existence ends up being the instrument by which the world is saved.

## Key Facts

- **Original name:** Sméagol, of the Stoor branch of hobbits. Before the Ring, he was young and curious and fond of roots and beginnings of things. [book_lotr]
- **The murder:** The Ring was found by Déagol, his companion, while fishing. Sméagol saw it and wanted it. He killed Déagol for it. He had owned it for an hour. [book_lotr]
- **Gollum:** The name comes from the sound he makes in his throat — a swallowing, gurgling noise. He was called Gollum by others and eventually became it. [book_lotr]
- **Age:** Approximately 580 years old at the time of the War of the Ring, sustained far beyond natural life by the Ring's prolonging power. Without the Ring, he aged rapidly. [book_lotr]
- **The split self:** The Gollum/Sméagol internal division is Tolkien's portrayal of the Ring's corruption — not possession but fragmentation. Sméagol retains, barely, the possibility of goodness. Gollum has surrendered entirely. The struggle between them is the residue of what he was. [book_lotr]
- **His role in the quest:** He guides Frodo and Sam through Emyn Muil and the Dead Marshes to Mordor. He knows the paths. He also plots to lead them into Shelob's lair. Both things are simultaneously true. [book_lotr]

## How He Lost the Ring

Bilbo found the Ring in the tunnels under the Misty Mountains — it had slipped from Gollum's finger (the Ring's first abandonment of a bearer it had used up). Bilbo did not know what it was. He pocketed it. Gollum led him, unknowingly, to the exit of the mountain in the course of losing his riddling game. Bilbo could have killed Gollum as he fled, but chose mercy — pity stayed his hand. The Ring was passed, and Gollum spent sixty years searching for it. [book_lotr] [book_hobbit] [film_fellowship]

## The Servant and the Schemer

Frodo shows Gollum mercy at the Emyn Muil — does not let Sam kill him, accepts him as guide. Gollum responds to this mercy with something approaching genuine feeling — the buried Sméagol briefly emerges, calls Frodo "master," and tries to serve truly. This is not performance. It is the last flicker of what the Ring has not yet entirely destroyed.

Sam does not trust him. Sam is correct that he is dangerous. Sam is also not entirely wrong that he is pitiable. Both things are true simultaneously, and Tolkien does not resolve the tension. [book_lotr] [film_two_towers]

## The End at the Crack of Doom

At Sammath Naur, when Frodo claims the Ring instead of destroying it, Gollum attacks and bites off Frodo's finger. He recovers his Precious, dances in mad triumph on the edge of the Crack of Doom, and falls in. He dies with what he loved. The Ring is destroyed.

Tolkien's letter is explicit: Gollum's fall was providential. Frodo's will had failed; the Ring could not be destroyed by its bearer's choice. The mercy shown to Gollum by Bilbo, by Frodo, allowed him to exist long enough to fulfill this role. The world was saved not by heroism but by pity. [book_lotr] [film_return_king]
