# Arwen Undómiel

## Overview

Arwen is the daughter of Elrond and Celebrían, granddaughter of Galadriel and Celeborn, and the most beautiful of the Elves remaining in Middle-earth in the Third Age. She is called Undómiel — Evenstar — the last and most beautiful of the Eldar as the Elder Days wane. She is also the person who makes the same choice Lúthien Tinúviel made in the First Age: to give up Elvish immortality for love of a mortal man, accepting the Gift of Men and the death that comes with it.

Her story is largely told in the Appendices rather than the main text — Tolkien placed it there deliberately, as a tale not fully told until afterward, like the last leaf of a closing book.

## Key Facts

- **Age:** She was born in the Third Age and is nearly 3,000 years old at the time of the War of the Ring. She has lived through most of the Third Age. [book_lotr]
- **Her choice:** She is given a choice by her nature — she is half-elven, as her father Elrond was, and may choose Elvish immortality or the mortal life of Men. She chooses mortality for Aragorn. This forfeits her passage to the Undying Lands. [book_lotr]
- **Her father's grief:** Elrond loves his daughter and knows what her choice means. He tells Aragorn that he will not give his daughter to a wandering Ranger; only the King of the Reunited Kingdom is worthy of her. This is not cruelty — he is giving Aragorn the standard to strive for while also delaying the moment he must give his daughter to mortality. [book_lotr]
- **The banner:** She makes the standard of the King that Aragorn bears at the Black Gate — the symbol of the return of the Dúnedain kings, woven by her hands. [book_lotr]
- **Film note:** The film trilogy expands Arwen's role significantly, making her presence active throughout rather than confined to key scenes and the Appendices. Her Rivendell role and the scene with the shards of Narsil are film expansions. [film_fellowship] [film_return_king]

## After the War

Arwen and Aragorn are married in Minas Tirith. She is the Queen of the Reunited Kingdom. After Aragorn's death — he chooses when to die, as the Dúnedain kings could — she goes to Lothlórien alone. It is diminished, fading, empty. She lies down on the hill of Cerin Amroth where she and Aragorn were first betrothed and dies there with the coming of winter. She has chosen mortal death and she embraces it, though not without grief. She is the last living thing the reader sees in Middle-earth before the tale fully closes. [book_lotr]
